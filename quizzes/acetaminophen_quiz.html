⚠️ 4-HOUR RULE (PCG 2.06)

Do not administer if the patient has received acetaminophen within the past 4 hours. Always ask sending facility about prior acetaminophen — including oral Tylenol. Even though dosing interval is q 6 hr, the 4-hour window is the safety check from PCG 2.06.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Analgesic effects: Believed to be due to activation of descending serotonergic inhibitory pathways in the CNS. Interactions with other nociceptive systems may be involved. Antipyresis: Produced from inhibition of the hypothalamic heat-regulating center. Note: this is a central mechanism — NOT peripheral prostaglandin inhibition like NSAIDs.

🤰 Pregnancy

Crosses the placenta. Considered appropriate for the treatment of pain and fever in pregnancy. One of the safest analgesic/antipyretic options available for pregnant patients. No dose reduction required. No fetal risks associated with NSAIDs (premature ductus closure) or opioids (neonatal abstinence).

  • Sapphire pump setup: General calculation mode → dose calculation (mg/mL) → weight-based for peds only → do NOT enter a value for dose → enter total mL for VTBI and 00:15 min for time. Use Handtevy or LFN dosing chart for VTBI.
  • Indicated for mild to moderate pain, patients unable/unwilling to receive narcotics, and/or as an adjunct with opioids for severe pain.
  • Should NOT be the sole agent used for post-intubation analgesia. The discomfort of intubation requires opioid-level analgesia; acetaminophen is an adjunct, not a substitute.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity; Severe hepatic disease. Note the distinction: severe hepatic DISEASE is a CI, while hepatic IMPAIRMENT is a caution.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Caution in patients with hepatic impairment (elevated LFTs, chronic alcohol use, malnutrition). Caution with hypotension — IV acetaminophen can worsen it.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (5)

Hypotension with rapid administration; hepatotoxicity; nausea/vomiting; headache; thrombocytopenia. The hypotension risk is why the 15-minute infusion time is mandatory, not a suggestion.

📋 Hepatic Impairment vs Severe Hepatic Disease

Hepatic impairment (elevated LFTs, chronic alcohol use) = CAUTION. Use clinical judgment, consider risk-benefit, consult medical direction. Severe hepatic disease (liver failure, severe cirrhosis, acute hepatitis) = CONTRAINDICATION. The distinction matters: many transport patients have some liver impairment — it doesn't automatically preclude acetaminophen.

⚠️ Hepatotoxicity Mechanism — NAPQI

Acetaminophen is 90% metabolized via glucuronidation/sulfation (safe). But 5–10% goes through CYP2E1 → NAPQI (toxic metabolite). Normally conjugated by glutathione. In chronic alcohol users: CYP2E1 is induced (more NAPQI) + glutathione depleted (less detox) = ↑ hepatotoxicity risk. The antidote is N-acetylcysteine (NAC, 12.57) which replenishes glutathione.
