⚠️ Second-Line Status — Per PCG 2.06
Hydromorphone is the second-line opioid agent if fentanyl is contraindicated or unavailable.
Fentanyl (12.30) is the preferred first-line opioid: faster onset (1-2 min vs 15 min), more predictable, weight-based, has infusion option. Use hydromorphone when fentanyl cannot be used.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Opioid agonist, principal agonist of mu-receptors, showing a weak affinity for kappa-receptors, binds to opiate receptors in spinal cord and CNS, altering perception of and response to painful stimuli while producing generalized CNS depression. Hydromorphone is a semi-synthetic opioid derived from morphine. It is 5-7× more potent than morphine mg-for-mg, which is why the doses are much smaller (0.5-1 mg vs morphine 2-5 mg).

🤰 Pregnancy

Hydromorphone crosses the placenta. When opioids are needed to treat acute pain in pregnant patients, the lowest effective dose for only the expected duration of pain should be prescribed.

⚖️ SC #1 — EQUIANALGESIC CONVERSION TABLE

1 mg hydromorphone = 75 mcg fentanyl = 5 mg morphine.

This conversion is critical for:
• Switching from fentanyl to hydromorphone mid-transport
• Calculating total opioid exposure across agents
• Communicating equivalent doses to receiving facility

Example: Patient received fentanyl 150 mcg total → hydromorphone equivalent = 150 ÷ 75 = 2 mg hydromorphone → morphine equivalent = 2 × 5 = 10 mg morphine.

🔥 SC #2 — BURN PATIENTS: Higher Doses Required

Burn patients may require much higher doses to achieve pain relief. Burns cause massive inflammatory mediator release, altered drug distribution (third-spacing, increased Vd), upregulated pain pathways, and possibly opioid tolerance in chronic burn patients. Standard doses often provide inadequate analgesia. Titrate aggressively using the pain scale while monitoring respiratory status. Per PCG 2.06: the goal is pain control to a point of tolerance while maintaining intact airway and respiratory drive.

📊 SC #3 — ETCO₂ MONITORING: Mandatory for Multiple Doses

ETCO₂ should be monitored in patients receiving multiple doses of opiates or receiving sedative agents. Also per PCG 2.06 Pearl #3: "ETCO₂ must be monitored in all patients receiving multiple doses of pain medications, any amount of ketamine, and those receiving analgesia infusions." ETCO₂ detects hypoventilation (rising CO₂) BEFORE SpO₂ drops — earlier warning of respiratory depression.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (1)

Hypersensitivity. Single, simple CI. Note: unlike some formulary drugs, there are no cardiac, respiratory, or metabolic CIs — the clinical cautions are in the warnings instead.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (2)

1. COPD patients: Hydromorphone is a respiratory depressant which may cause excessive hypercapnia. COPD patients already have impaired CO₂ elimination → opioid-induced respiratory depression → further CO₂ retention → respiratory acidosis → potential respiratory arrest.

2. Head injury patients: May cause an increase in ICP and hypotension. CO₂ retention → cerebral vasodilation → ↑ ICP. Hypotension → ↓ cerebral perfusion pressure. Both effects are dangerous in TBI.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (8)

Respiratory depression; flushing; headaches; general malaise; hypotension; hypertension; tremors; abuse and/or dependence of drug.

Respiratory depression is the most dangerous adverse reaction — can progress to apnea and death. This is why ETCO₂ monitoring is required for multiple doses. Reversed by naloxone (12.58): 0.4 mg IV/IM/IN, repeat q 3-5 min, titrated to respiratory function.

🔄 PCG 2.06 PAIN MANAGEMENT PEARLS — Cross-Reference

• Providers may decrease dose or prolong intervals to reduce adverse effects while achieving clinical goal
• Pre-administration of nausea medications should be considered
• Even in medication-tolerant patients, respiratory depression may occur after 2nd or 3rd dose
• Use caution combining narcotics + benzodiazepines → deep sedation and/or apnea
• Reduce dose in hypotensive/catecholamine-depleted states
