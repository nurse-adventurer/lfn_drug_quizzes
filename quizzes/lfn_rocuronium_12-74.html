🔍 IV vs IM Dose — The 1.8× Relationship

IV: 1 mg/kg (max 100 mg) — standard RSI dose; onset ~1 min
IM: 1.8 mg/kg (max 100 mg) — for when IV/IO access is unavailable; onset is slower (~3–6 min) and more variable than IV

The IM dose is 1.8× the IV dose to compensate for incomplete and slower absorption from muscle tissue. The max is the same (100 mg) for both routes.

For a 70 kg patient: IV = 70 mg; IM = 126 mg (but capped at 100 mg if the patient weight would exceed it). For a 56 kg patient: IV = 56 mg; IM = 101 mg — just over max, so IM dose = 100 mg.

The PCG 8.27 RSI protocol lists only the IV dose (1 mg/kg); the IM route is in the 12.74 formulary table only.

📋 Maintenance Dose: 0.5 mg/kg — What "May Repeat PRN" Means

The maintenance dose (0.5 mg/kg) is exactly half the IV RSI dose. It is used to re-establish or continue paralysis after the initial dose wears off (onset of paralysis recovery is typically 30+ min but varies). "May repeat PRN" means repeat as clinically indicated — there is no stated interval or total dose ceiling beyond the per-dose max of 100 mg.

Both adult and peds maintenance dose: "Same as Adult" (0.5 mg/kg, repeat PRN, max 100 mg). No route restriction stated — context implies IV/IO for ongoing paralysis of an intubated patient.

🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Acts by binding competitively to cholinergic receptors at the motor end plate to antagonize the action of acetylcholine, an effect that is reversible in the presence of acetylcholinesterase inhibitors, such as neostigmine and edrophonium."

📝 MOA — Key Concepts

Competitive binding: Rocuronium occupies the cholinergic (nicotinic) receptor at the motor end plate without activating it — it simply blocks acetylcholine from binding. This is "competitive" because the drug competes with ACh for the same receptor site.

Does NOT depolarize: Because it blocks without activating, no muscle fasciculations occur (compare to succinylcholine, which first depolarizes causing fasciculations before sustained paralysis).

Reversibility: The LFN MOA explicitly names the reversal mechanism — acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (neostigmine and edrophonium) increase synaptic ACh concentration to competitively displace rocuronium. Note: sugammadex (Bridion) is the modern selective reversal agent for rocuronium, but it is not mentioned in LFN 12.74's MOA text — the LFN specifically names neostigmine and edrophonium.

Category: "Non-depolarizing" distinguishes from succinylcholine (12.78), which IS a depolarizing agent.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

"Rocuronium crosses the placenta. However, if indicated, it may be used in pregnancy."

Minimal LFN 12.74 pregnancy text — shortest framework: acknowledge placental transfer, then endorse use when indicated. No specific fetal risk elaboration. Compare to propofol (12.72), which specified "neonatal CNS and respiratory depression." Rocuronium's placental transfer produces the same potential effect (fetal paralysis/respiratory depression) but LFN 12.74 does not enumerate it — the message is simply: crosses placenta, use when needed.

📋 Special Considerations (1)
1
Paralytic agent of choice if patient age < 10 years.

LFN 12.74 has exactly one SC. It establishes a clear age-based preference. Children under 10 receive rocuronium rather than succinylcholine due to succinylcholine's risk of acute rhabdomyolysis and hyperkalemia in this age group (particularly in patients with undiagnosed myopathies that are more prevalent in this age group).

LFN 12.78 (succinylcholine) SC #1 echoes this: "Rocuronium is the paralytic agent of choice in pediatric patients < 10 yo. Except for emergency intubation or instances where immediate securing of the airway is necessary (e.g., laryngospasm)." This exception acknowledges succinylcholine's faster onset and shorter duration as advantages in true crash airway situations even in young children.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATION (1)

Hypersensitivity — only one CI listed in LFN 12.74. This is notably minimal compared to succinylcholine (12.78), which has 9 contraindications. Rocuronium's non-depolarizing mechanism avoids the potassium-releasing and malignant hyperthermia risks that generate most of succinylcholine's CIs.

⚠️ Warning / Precautions (1 — with mandatory language)

"Administer carefully, have resuscitation equipment at the bedside. This drug WILL cause apnea."

Two directives and one certain statement:
• Administer carefully — general caution
• Have resuscitation equipment at the bedside — preparation requirement
• "WILL cause apnea" — not "can cause," not "may cause" — definitive certainty

The word "WILL" reflects the intended mechanism of RSI: rocuronium paralysis includes respiratory muscle paralysis → apnea is expected and certain, not a risk. This is the mechanism of controlled airway management — the apnea is the point. Compare to propofol (12.72) which says "can cause apnea" — a risk. Rocuronium says "WILL cause apnea" — a certainty, because skeletal muscle paralysis includes the diaphragm.

⚠️ Adverse Reactions (2 only)
Apnea
Respiratory muscle paralysis — expected and certain; also stated in the warning
Paralysis
Skeletal muscle paralysis — the drug's intended primary effect, also listed as an adverse reaction

Only two adverse reactions listed. Both are also the drug's mechanism of action — rocuronium's adverse reactions ARE its effects. This reflects that the only way rocuronium can harm is by doing what it is designed to do (paralysis and apnea) in a patient who does not have an established, secured airway. The LFN adverse reaction list for rocuronium is the most minimal of any drug in the formulary requiring such high-stakes clinical decision-making.
