🩸 ESOPHAGEAL VARICES — TRANSPORT CONTEXT
Esophageal varices are dilated submucosal veins in the esophagus caused by portal hypertension — most commonly from cirrhosis. Elevated portal pressure is transmitted to the esophageal venous plexus → varices form. Rupture → massive upper GI hemorrhage with high mortality.

Octreotide works by reducing splanchnic blood flow and portal pressure (via suppression of vasodilatory peptides), reducing bleeding from variceal rupture while the patient is transported to definitive endoscopic treatment.

🩸 Why Octreotide for Esophageal Varices?

Somatostatin (endogenous) and its synthetic analog octreotide act on splanchnic vasculature indirectly by suppressing vasodilatory peptides — particularly glucagon and serotonin. Without these vasodilators, splanchnic arterioles constrict → splanchnic blood flow decreases → portal venous inflow decreases → portal pressure falls → variceal wall tension decreases → bleeding slows or stops.

Octreotide does not directly constrict vessels — it works by removing the vasodilatory signal. This is a key distinction from vasopressin (12.83), which directly constricts splanchnic vasculature via V1 receptors.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Octreotide crosses the placenta and can be detected in the newborn at delivery. Data are limited. Based on available case reports in patients with acromegaly who received normal doses during pregnancy, an increased risk of congenital malformations has not been observed.

Acromegaly reference: GH-secreting tumors are one of the LFN-listed warnings for octreotide, making this pregnancy note internally consistent — the same patient population studied for pregnancy safety.

💉 Bolus-Then-Infusion Dosing Structure

Octreotide's LFN dosing is a two-part sequential regimen:

Part 1 — Bolus: 25–50 mcg slow IVP. The "slow" qualifier matters — rapid IV push of somatostatin analogs can cause bradycardia and conduction abnormalities (listed as adverse reactions). Administering as a slow push reduces the cardiac adverse effect risk at bolus.

Part 2 — Infusion: 25–50 mcg/hr continuous infusion following the bolus. This maintains steady-state splanchnic vasodilation suppression during transport. The infusion is necessary because octreotide's duration is only up to 90 minutes — without the infusion, the bolus effect will wear off before the patient reaches definitive treatment.

Both the bolus and infusion use the same mcg range (25–50), giving flexibility based on patient hemodynamic status and bleeding severity.

📊 Glucose Monitoring — Bidirectional Risk

LFN 12.64 Warnings explicitly state: "Can cause hypo or hyperglycemia." This bidirectional glucose dysregulation is mechanistically explained by the MOA:

↑ HYPERGLYCEMIA
Mechanism: Octreotide suppresses insulin secretion from pancreatic beta cells. Less insulin → less glucose uptake → blood glucose rises.

Most common with acute dosing. Relevant in diabetic patients and those already hyperglycemic.
↓ HYPOGLYCEMIA
Mechanism: Octreotide also suppresses glucagon secretion from pancreatic alpha cells. Less glucagon → less hepatic glucose release → blood glucose falls.

More common with longer use. Particularly relevant in patients who are fasting or malnourished (common in cirrhosis).

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (1)

Hypersensitivity to octreotide or any component. Single CI — reflects that octreotide's main risks arise from its peptide suppression effects and are managed through warnings rather than hard contraindications.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (8 categories + glucose note)
Biliary Disease
Suppression of cholecystokinin → ↓ gallbladder motility → cholelithiasis risk (also listed as adverse reaction)
GH-Secreting Tumors (Acromegaly)
Primary therapeutic use — dosing requirements differ from emergency dosing; relevant to monitoring
Diabetes Mellitus
Bidirectional glucose effect — insulin and glucagon suppression; monitor BGL closely
Thyroid Disease
Octreotide can cause hypothyroidism (listed as adverse reaction); caution with existing thyroid dysfunction
Cardiac Disease
Bradycardia, conduction abnormalities, arrhythmias — all listed as adverse reactions; significant concern with pre-existing cardiac disease
Hepatic Impairment
Reduced clearance — patients with cirrhosis (the primary octreotide patient) may have prolonged drug effect
Renal Impairment
Reduced clearance; dose adjustment may be needed in severe renal failure
Elderly Patients
Increased sensitivity; higher risk of adverse effects at standard doses
⚠️ GLUCOSE WARNING (explicit in LFN 12.64)
"Can cause hypo or hyperglycemia" — this bidirectional warning is explicitly stated as part of the warnings section, not just an adverse reaction. It reflects the dual peptide suppression (insulin → hyperglycemia; glucagon → hypoglycemia) and is clinically important in the cirrhotic patient population who may already have abnormal glucose regulation.

The cardiac adverse reactions (bradycardia, conduction abnormalities, arrhythmias) are the most clinically acute concern in the transport setting. ⚠️ Key Points

  • CI (1): Hypersensitivity only
  • Warnings (8+): Biliary disease; GH-secreting tumors; diabetes mellitus; thyroid disease; cardiac disease; hepatic impairment; renal impairment; elderly. Can cause hypo OR hyperglycemia.
  • Adverse — Cardiac: Bradycardia; conduction abnormalities; arrhythmias; edema
  • Adverse — GI: N/V; diarrhea; abdominal pain; cholelithiasis; fat malabsorption
  • Adverse — Metabolic: Hyperglycemia; hypoglycemia; hypothyroidism; flushing
  • Adverse — Neuro: Dizziness; drowsiness; fatigue; headache; weakness; vision disturbances
  • Slow IVP required: Rapid bolus → bradycardia/conduction abnormalities
  • Glucose mechanism: ↓ Insulin → hyperglycemia; ↓ Glucagon → hypoglycemia
  • Hepatic impairment warning: Relevant — cirrhotic patients are the primary population
  • Infusion needed: Duration up to 90 min — bolus alone insufficient for transport
