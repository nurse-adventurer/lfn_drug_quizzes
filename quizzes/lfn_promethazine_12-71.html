⚠️ Adult Upper Bound: 50 mg (Not 25 mg)

LFN 12.71 adult dose range is 12.5–50 mg. The upper bound of 50 mg is frequently confused with the IV concentration limit (25 mg/mL) or the peds max dose (25 mg). These are three distinct values: dose range goes up to 50 mg; IV concentration cap is 25 mg/mL; peds max is 25 mg. The IV rate limit is expressed as a rate (<25 mg/min), not as a time duration.

The word "deep" before "IM" is in both adult and peds entries. Deep IM is specified to reduce the risk of inadvertent IV injection and to ensure adequate absorption from muscle tissue (not subcutaneous).

🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Competes with histamine for H1 receptor site. Blocks cholinergic receptors in the vomiting center which may mediate nausea and vomiting."

📝 MOA in Clinical Context

LFN 12.71 describes a two-part MOA: (1) H1 receptor competition — promethazine competes with histamine at the H1 receptor, explaining antihistamine and antiemetic effects. (2) Cholinergic receptor blockade in the vomiting center — reduces the muscarinic input that contributes to nausea/vomiting signaling.

Notably, LFN 12.71 does not describe dopamine (D2) receptor antagonism in the MOA field, even though promethazine is a phenothiazine with known D2 activity. The LFN pharmacologic category is simply "Antiemetic" — not "phenothiazine" or "antipsychotic." For LFN-specific study, the MOA is: H1 competition + cholinergic blockade in vomiting center.

The cholinergic receptor blockade (not labeled "anticholinergic" in LFN language) also explains the adverse reactions: over-sedation reflects CNS H1 and cholinergic receptor blockade; delirium reflects central anticholinergic effects especially in elderly.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

"Promethazine crosses the placenta. Promethazine is indicated for use during labor for obstetric sedation and may be used alone or as an adjunct to opioid analgesics. Promethazine may be used as adjunctive therapy in the management of nausea and vomiting of pregnancy when the preferred agents do not provide initial symptom improvement or when symptoms persist despite other therapies."

Two distinct pregnancy uses: (1) Obstetric sedation during labor — indicated, may be used alone or with opioids. (2) NVP adjunct therapy — only when preferred agents fail or symptoms persist despite other therapies (not first-line for NVP). Compare: diphenhydramine 12.22 pregnancy language is essentially the same structure — adjunct when preferred agents fail.

📋 Special Considerations (3)
1
Reduce dose in the elderly and/or in patients receiving concomitant sedating medications. (Also listed as a Warning: "use with caution in the elderly or when giving other sedating medications.")
2
Dystonic reactions may be treated with diphenhydramine (Benadryl). Note: LFN 12.71 specifies diphenhydramine only — not benztropine or other anticholinergics. PCG 2.05 Pearl #4 echoes: ondansetron is drug of choice for dystonic reactions due to promethazine (complementary — treat EPS with diphenhydramine per formulary, ondansetron per PCG).
3
NS, D5, or LR may be used to dilute prior to IV administration. Three acceptable diluents. This is the only LFN formulary source for acceptable diluents for promethazine IV.
⚠️ IV Extravasation — LFN Warning Language

LFN 12.71 Warning #1 reads: "Check patency of IV lines prior to administration due to high risk of necrosis with extravasation." This is LFN-specific language — the key elements are: (1) check patency before giving, (2) rationale is high risk of necrosis, (3) the trigger is extravasation.

The LFN 12.71 warning does not use "vesicant," "black box warning," or specify amputation/gangrene risk — those are clinical pharmacology descriptions not in the LFN formulary text. For LFN-specific study: the warning is about patency check + necrosis risk from extravasation. The IV concentration (≤25 mg/mL) and rate (<25 mg/min) constraints are in the dose table, not the warning section.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Children <2 years — listed in the dose table ("Do not use in children <2 years") and confirmed in the CI section. Risk: fatal respiratory depression.

2. Hypersensitivity — to promethazine or any component.

Note: The pediatric CI (<2 years) appears in both the dosing table and the CI section — it is a hard stop, not merely a precaution. No weight-based exception is listed in the LFN 12.71 formulary CI section (the PCG adds a ">10 kg" qualifier).

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (3)

1. Check patency of IV lines prior to administration due to high risk of necrosis with extravasation.

2. Use with caution in the elderly or when giving other sedating medications.

3. Recent alcohol use.

Three warnings total. The extravasation/necrosis warning is the primary safety driver for IV administration technique. Elderly and sedating medication caution reflects the enhanced sedation and delirium risk. Recent alcohol use reflects additive CNS depression.
