🔬 Mechanism of Action

Produces centrally mediated sympatholytic, sedative, and analgesic effects (selective alpha-2 adrenergic agonist). Acts primarily on alpha-2 receptors in the locus coeruleus (brainstem arousal center), mimicking natural sleep pathways. Produces "cooperative sedation" — patients can be aroused to follow commands, then return to sleep. Also provides analgesia via spinal cord alpha-2 receptors. The sympatholytic effect (↓ sympathetic outflow) explains the bradycardia and hypotension.

😴 WHY "COOPERATIVE SEDATION" MATTERS

Unlike GABA-ergic sedatives (propofol, benzodiazepines), dexmedetomidine produces sedation that resembles natural stage 2 NREM sleep. Patients can be awakened with gentle stimulation for neurological exams, then drift back to comfortable sedation. This is invaluable for neuro patients requiring frequent GCS/pupil checks. Minimal respiratory depression means patients maintain airway reflexes and spontaneous ventilation — useful during ventilator weaning.

🤰 Pregnancy

Dexmedetomidine crosses the placenta. Bradycardia is reported with use. Fetal bradycardia was noted following a maternal IV bolus dose during surgery. Use with caution in pregnant patients — the fetal bradycardia risk is especially relevant for OB patients.

  • Will typically require supplemental sedation medications for transport if it is the sole medication used for ongoing sedation. Dexmedetomidine alone often provides insufficient sedation for the stimulation of transport (vibration, noise, movement). Consider adding propofol, ketamine, or benzodiazepine as needed.
  • Should not be abruptly discontinued, especially when given at high doses, as it can cause withdrawal symptoms. Taper gradually — reduce by 0.1–0.2 mcg/kg/hr q 30–60 min. Abrupt d/c after prolonged use → rebound sympathetic activation: hypertension, tachycardia, agitation.
  • Bradycardia, sinus arrest, and hypotension can occur. Treat by decreasing or stopping the infusion, IV fluids, and use of vasopressors. Bradycardia and sinus arrest are usually due to high vagal tone and respond to atropine.
🚨 SC #3 — SINUS ARREST is Listed

This is one of the few LFN drugs that explicitly lists sinus arrest as both a warning (SC #3) and an adverse reaction. Sinus arrest = complete cessation of SA node activity → no P waves, no heart rate. Usually due to high vagal tone at higher doses. Responds to atropine. This is why ECG monitoring is mandatory during dexmedetomidine infusion.

📋 PCG 2.08 PEARLS — Key Transport Context

Per PCG 2.08 Pearls: all patients receiving sedatives require continuous ECG, BP, HR, SpO₂, ETCO₂ monitoring. Pain/sedation meds should be reduced in hypotension (while aggressively treating the hypotension). Chemically paralyzed patients should NOT have RASS documented — write "Chemically Paralyzed" under GCS qualifier. Paralyzed patients may need higher-end sedation doses (BP permitting).

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity. Simple, single CI.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (Extensive)

Bradycardia may occur with high maintenance doses. Use with caution in: heart blocks, bradycardia, severe vascular dysfunction, hypovolemia, or chronic hypertension. Bradycardia and hypotension may be more pronounced in elderly patients or patients with diabetes mellitus. Reduced dosing recommended for patients with hepatic impairment (hepatically metabolized).

🚫 Adverse Reactions (5)

Hypotension; bradycardia; sinus arrest; respiratory depression; withdrawal symptoms (with abrupt d/c).

The most critical: sinus arrest (treat with atropine) and withdrawal symptoms (taper, don't stop abruptly). Respiratory depression is listed but is generally minimal compared to GABA-ergic sedatives — a relative advantage of dexmedetomidine.
