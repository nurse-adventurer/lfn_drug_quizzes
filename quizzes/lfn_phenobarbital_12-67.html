🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Alters sensory cortex, cerebellar, and motor activities; produces sedation, hypnosis, and anesthesia."

Sensory Cortex
Depresses sensory cortex activity → ↓ pain perception and sensory input processing
Cerebellar
Alters cerebellar function → ataxia, incoordination at sedating doses
Motor Activity
Suppresses motor cortex → reduces seizure propagation and motor manifestations
Sedation → Hypnosis → Anesthesia
Dose-dependent CNS depression spectrum from sedation through hypnosis to anesthesia
📝 LFN MOA vs. Pharmacology Context

The LFN 12.67 MOA describes the clinical/anatomic effect ("alters sensory cortex, cerebellar, and motor activities") rather than the molecular mechanism. Pharmacologically, barbiturates enhance GABA-A receptor activity (increasing chloride channel open duration) and at higher doses can directly activate the channel without GABA. This is distinct from benzodiazepines (which increase channel opening frequency). The LFN MOA framing is what appears in the formulary and is what should be referenced in protocol questions.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations (Extensive)

"Phenobarbital crosses the placenta. Fetal and maternal blood concentrations may be similar following parenteral administration. An increased incidence of fetal abnormalities may occur following maternal use.

When used during the third trimester of pregnancy, withdrawal symptoms may occur in the neonate, including seizures and hyperirritability; symptoms of withdrawal may be delayed in the neonate up to 14 days after birth.

Use during labor does not impair uterine activity; however, respiratory depression may occur in the newborn; resuscitation equipment should be available, especially for premature infants.

Use for the treatment of epilepsy should be avoided during pregnancy."

Key points: (1) Fetal drug levels approach maternal levels. (2) Fetal abnormalities risk. (3) Neonatal withdrawal can be delayed up to 14 days. (4) Avoid for epilepsy treatment in pregnancy — but may still be used emergently for SE. (5) Have resuscitation equipment for premature newborns.

💉 Infusion Rate: Why 60 mg/min Matters

The LFN 12.67 specified rate is 60 mg/min — not the 50–100 mg/min range sometimes cited in general pharmacology references. Exceeding this rate significantly increases cardiovascular risk: rapid phenobarbital infusion causes vasodilation and myocardial depression → hypotension → cardiovascular collapse in compromised patients. The 60 mg/min rate is a deliberate, conservative ceiling.

At 60 mg/min, a 1000 mg dose (the max) takes approximately 16–17 minutes to infuse. For a 40 kg pediatric patient receiving 20 mg/kg = 800 mg ÷ 60 mg/min = approximately 13 minutes. The infusion is never a fast push — it always requires an IV pump or careful manual rate control.

⏱️ Onset 5 min / Duration >6 Hours — Clinical Implications

Onset of 5 minutes is slower than benzodiazepines (lorazepam acts in 1–2 min IV). This means phenobarbital is not the first drug drawn in a seizing patient — it is a second or third-line agent once faster-acting drugs have been given or have failed.

Duration of >6 hours provides sustained anticonvulsant coverage. This is clinically important for transport: a patient loaded with phenobarbital has ongoing seizure suppression throughout the transport period. However, it also means prolonged sedation and respiratory depression monitoring requirements — CNS and respiratory effects do not resolve quickly after administration.

💊 Trade Name: Sezaby — Not Luminal

LFN 12.67 lists the trade name as Sezaby — the FDA-approved IV formulation of phenobarbital for neonates, approved in 2020. Luminal is a historical trade name for oral phenobarbital (no longer widely available in the US). In the LFN formulary context, Sezaby is the correct trade name. Confusing the two is a common error in study materials — use Sezaby for any LFN 12.67-specific question.

👶 Pediatric Paradoxical Reactions

LFN 12.67 Warning explicitly notes: "May cause paradoxical responses including agitation and hyperactivity in pediatrics." This is not unique to phenobarbital — paradoxical CNS stimulation with barbiturates and benzodiazepines in pediatric patients is a recognized phenomenon, particularly in children under 6 years. The mechanism involves disinhibition of CNS circuits. Clinically: a child who becomes more agitated or hyperactive after phenobarbital is not failing treatment — they may be experiencing a paradoxical response, not insufficient dosing. Recognizing this prevents dose escalation that could push the child into true respiratory depression.

⚡ Status Epilepticus — Drug Line Context
Benzodiazepines (lorazepam, midazolam, diazepam) → First-line for all SE
Levetiracetam / Fosphenytoin / Valproate → Second-line
Phenobarbital 12.67 → Third-line / Refractory SE
Ketamine / Propofol / Barbiturate coma → Fourth-line refractory

Phenobarbital's position: After benzos and at least one second-line agent have been given or failed. Its long duration makes it valuable for transport coverage once SE is broken — seizure recurrence risk is low with active phenobarbital on board.
🚫 CONTRAINDICATION (1)

Hypersensitivity — hypersensitivity to phenobarbital or any component of the formulation. Single CI; notably broader conditions (hepatic failure, respiratory compromise) are listed as warnings, not contraindications.

Respiratory depression is the most immediately life-threatening adverse reaction in the transport context. Risk is highest after benzodiazepines have already been given (additive CNS depression). Any patient receiving phenobarbital requires continuous monitoring of respiratory rate and SpO₂, with airway equipment immediately available.
