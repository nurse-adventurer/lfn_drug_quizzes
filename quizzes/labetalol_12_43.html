Mechanism of Action

Selectively antagonizes alpha-1 adrenergic receptors and non-selectively antagonizes beta-1 and beta-2 adrenergic receptors. This dual blockade provides vasodilation (alpha-1) and heart rate control (beta-1) without the reflex tachycardia seen with pure vasodilators.

Adverse Reactions

Dizziness, fatigue, hypotension, bradycardia, worsening CHF, bronchospasms. Beta-2 blockade can precipitate severe bronchospasm in susceptible patients.

  1. Max total dose is 300 mg / 24 hours for ALL indications. Track cumulative dosing across boluses and infusions.
  2. Preeclampsia dose escalation: Double the previous dose every 10 minutes (20→40→80 mg) until goal BP of SBP 140–150 / DBP 90–100 is met.
  3. Post-thrombolytic target: BP 180/105. Excessive reduction increases risk of hemorrhagic conversion. Start with 10 mg slow IVP followed by 2–8 mg/min infusion.
  4. Pre/non-thrombolytic candidates with SBP >220 or DBP >120: Goal is 15% BP reduction, not normalization.
  5. Hemorrhagic stroke: Use when nicardipine alone is insufficient. 10–20 mg IV, repeat 20–40 mg q 10 min. Consider 1–2 mg/min infusion.
  6. Infusion mixture: Remove 40 mL from 100 mL bag before adding 200 mg labetalol = 2 mg/mL concentration.
🤰 Pregnancy Context — Preeclampsia / Eclampsia

IV labetalol is recommended for management of hypertensive emergencies in pregnant or postpartum patients, including acute-onset severe hypertension in preeclampsia/eclampsia.

BP goal: SBP 140–150, DBP 90–100. This range maintains adequate placental perfusion while preventing hypertensive complications.

Labetalol crosses the placenta. The doubling dose escalation (20→40→80 mg) provides rapid BP control while allowing assessment of individual response between doses.

🧠 Stroke Context — Key Differences

Hemorrhagic stroke: Use as adjunct when nicardipine alone is insufficient. Lower starting dose (10–20 mg) with repeat 20–40 mg q 10 min.

Post-thrombolytic: Target 180/105. Start 10 mg slow IVP, then 2–8 mg/min infusion. Higher infusion rate reflects urgency of maintaining target.

Pre/non-thrombolytic: Goal is only 15% reduction. Aggressive lowering may worsen ischemic penumbra.

🫀 Aortic Emergency Context

Dual blockade is ideal for aortic emergencies: Alpha-1 blockade provides vasodilation to reduce BP, while beta blockade prevents reflex tachycardia and reduces aortic wall shear stress (dP/dt).

Starting dose: 20 mg IV with repeat 20–80 mg q 10 min. Higher repeat dose range reflects the critical need for rapid BP control in aortic dissection.

Pregnancy Considerations

Labetalol crosses the placenta. IV labetalol is recommended for use in management of hypertensive emergencies in pregnant or postpartum patients (including acute-onset severe hypertension in preeclampsia/eclampsia).

🚨 CONTRAINDICATION — Bronchial Asthma

Contraindicated in patients with bronchial asthma. Non-selective beta-2 blockade causes bronchoconstriction, which can precipitate severe, life-threatening bronchospasm. This is an absolute contraindication — do not administer regardless of dose adjustment.

🚨 CONTRAINDICATION — Uncontrolled Cardiac Failure

Contraindicated in patients with uncontrolled cardiac failure. Beta blockade reduces contractility and cardiac output. In decompensated heart failure, this can worsen pulmonary congestion and precipitate cardiogenic shock.

🚨 CONTRAINDICATION — Heart Block > First Degree

Contraindicated in patients with heart block greater than first degree. Beta-1 blockade further depresses AV conduction and can progress to complete heart block or asystole.

🚨 CONTRAINDICATION — Cardiogenic Shock & Severe Bradycardia

Contraindicated in cardiogenic shock and severe bradycardia. Beta blockade reduces heart rate and contractility, worsening hemodynamic instability in already compromised patients.

🚨 DO NOT USE — IV Calcium Channel Blockers

Do NOT use labetalol with IV calcium channel blockers (e.g., diltiazem, verapamil). Both drug classes depress cardiac function and cause vasodilation. Combined effect can cause profound hypotension, severe bradycardia, and potentially fatal cardiac depression.

⚠️ Non-Allergic Bronchospastic Disease (COPD)

Use with caution in patients with COPD or other non-allergic bronchospastic disease. While not an absolute contraindication like asthma, beta-2 blockade may still worsen bronchospasm. Monitor respiratory status closely.

⚠️ Well-Compensated Heart Failure

Use with caution in well-compensated patients with history of heart failure. While uncontrolled CHF is a contraindication, stable patients may tolerate labetalol with careful monitoring. Watch for signs of decompensation.

⚠️ Pheochromocytoma

Use with caution in patients with pheochromocytoma. While labetalol has alpha-blocking properties, unopposed alpha stimulation may still occur if beta blockade is disproportionate. Ensure adequate alpha blockade is established first.
