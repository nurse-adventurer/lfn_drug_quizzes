📊 Onset / Peak / Duration

Onset: 2–5 min  |  Peak: 5–15 min  |  Duration: 2–18 hours
Rapid onset allows titration to effect within minutes. Very long duration (up to 18 hrs) means cumulative dosing can produce prolonged hypotension — monitor closely and respect the 300 mg/24 hr max.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Selectively antagonizes alpha-1 adrenergic receptors; antagonizes beta-1 and beta-2 adrenergic receptors (selective alpha and non-selective beta blocker). The alpha-1 blockade produces vasodilation (lowers SVR), while beta blockade reduces heart rate and myocardial contractility — providing combined BP reduction without reflex tachycardia.

⚠️ Adverse Reactions

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Labetalol crosses the placenta. IV labetalol is recommended for use in the management of hypertensive emergencies in pregnant or postpartum patients (including acute-onset severe hypertension in preeclampsia/eclampsia).

  • Five distinct indications with different dosing: Aortic emergencies, hemorrhagic stroke (adjunct to nicardipine), post-thrombolytic therapy, pre/non-thrombolytic candidates, and preeclampsia/eclampsia/PIH. Each has unique starting doses, repeat intervals, and BP goals.
  • Universal max: 300 mg / 24 hours. This ceiling applies across ALL indications. Track cumulative dosing carefully, especially when transitioning from boluses to infusion.
  • Preeclampsia uses a doubling escalation: Start 20 mg → double q 10 min (20 → 40 → 80 → 80...) until goal SBP 140–150 and DBP 90–100. This is unique among the 5 indications.
  • Hemorrhagic stroke — second-line after nicardipine: Labetalol is used only "if BP not controlled by nicardipine alone." Nicardipine (12.59) is first-line for hemorrhagic stroke BP management.
  • Pregnancy-preferred agent: IV labetalol is specifically RECOMMENDED for hypertensive emergencies in pregnancy (preeclampsia/eclampsia). This makes it one of the safest antihypertensives in pregnancy per LFN formulary.
  • Infusion mixture preparation: 200 mg / 100 mL. Per LFN 12.01 footnote: remove 40 mL from a 100 mL bag prior to adding labetalol to make final concentration of 2 mg/mL.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Bronchial asthma; uncontrolled cardiac failure; heart block (greater than first degree); cardiogenic shock; severe bradycardia.

🚫 DO NOT Use with IV Calcium Channel Blockers

Per Warnings/Precautions: Do not use with IV calcium channel blockers. Combined use can cause severe bradycardia, hypotension, and heart failure.

⚠️ Caution — High-Risk Populations

Non-allergic bronchospastic disease (COPD); well-compensated patients with history of heart failure; pheochromocytoma.

⚠️ Adverse Reactions

Dizziness, fatigue, hypotension, bradycardia, worsening CHF, bronchospasms. The beta-2 blockade can precipitate bronchospasm — this is why asthma is a contraindication and COPD requires caution.

🤰 Pregnancy — RECOMMENDED Agent

Labetalol crosses the placenta. IV labetalol is recommended for hypertensive emergencies in pregnant or postpartum patients including acute-onset severe hypertension in preeclampsia/eclampsia. This is one of the FEW antihypertensives specifically recommended (not just "may be used") in pregnancy.

📋 Asthma vs COPD — Key Distinction

Asthma = CONTRAINDICATED (absolute). COPD = Caution (relative). Labetalol blocks beta-2 receptors which mediate bronchodilation. In asthma, this can trigger severe, life-threatening bronchospasm. In COPD, the risk exists but is lower — use with caution.
