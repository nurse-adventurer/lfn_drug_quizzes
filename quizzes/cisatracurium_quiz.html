🔬 Mechanism of Action

Blocks neural transmission at the myoneural junction by binding with cholinergic receptor sites. Cisatracurium competitively binds nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at the motor endplate, preventing ACh from triggering muscle contraction. It is a nondepolarizing agent — unlike succinylcholine, it does NOT depolarize the membrane first (no fasciculations, no hyperkalemia risk).

🧪 HOFMANN ELIMINATION — Why Cisatracurium Is Special

Cisatracurium undergoes Hofmann elimination — a spontaneous, organ-independent chemical degradation that occurs at physiologic pH and temperature. This means it does NOT depend on the liver or kidneys for metabolism. This makes cisatracurium's duration predictable even in patients with hepatic or renal failure. Rocuronium and vecuronium, by contrast, depend on hepatic metabolism and renal excretion, making their duration less predictable in organ dysfunction.

🤰 Pregnancy

Recommendations for sustained NMB in pregnant critically ill patients are not available due to insufficient evidence. Use with caution in preeclampsia/eclampsia — cisatracurium may be enhanced by concomitant medications (particularly MgSO₄). Preparations may contain benzyl alcohol — avoid in pregnant patients due to association with gasping syndrome in premature infants.

📋 No Numbered Special Considerations in LFN 12.14

Cisatracurium's formulary entry has no numbered SCs. However, critical clinical application principles apply from the post-intubation sedation PCG (2.08) and standard NMB practice:

🚨 CRITICAL — ALWAYS SEDATE BEFORE PARALYZING

Cisatracurium has NO sedative, anxiolytic, or analgesic properties. A paralyzed but unsedated patient is fully conscious, unable to move or communicate, and experiences profound psychological trauma. Per PCG 2.08: chemically paralyzed patients should receive sedation at the higher end of dose ranges (BP permitting). Minimum propofol infusion for paralyzed patients = 25 mcg/kg/min. RASS scoring is not applicable during paralysis — document "Chemically Paralyzed" under GCS qualifier.

📋 BOLUS BRIDGE — If Infusion Pump Fails

If the infusion pump fails during transport, cisatracurium can be maintained with intermittent boluses (0.1–0.2 mg/kg) every 35–45 minutes (the bolus duration). This is less smooth than infusion but provides coverage until the pump is fixed or you arrive at destination. Communicate the pump failure to the receiving facility.

📋 PREECLAMPSIA/ECLAMPSIA — MgSO₄ Interaction

MgSO₄ (used for eclampsia) potentiates neuromuscular blockade. Patients on magnesium infusions may have significantly prolonged and enhanced paralysis with cisatracurium. Use lower doses and expect longer duration. Per pregnancy considerations: "the action of cisatracurium may be enhanced by concomitant medications."

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity to cisatracurium or any component of the formulation. Note: no cross-reactivity CI with succinylcholine (different drug class). Benzylisoquinolinium agents may cross-react with each other.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (3)

1. Severe anaphylaxis has been reported. NMB agents are among the most common causes of perioperative anaphylaxis.
2. Bradycardia. Can occur during or after administration. Treat with atropine if symptomatic.
3. Patients with neuromuscular diseases may report residual paralysis. Myasthenia gravis, Lambert-Eaton, muscular dystrophies — use lowest effective dose, expect prolonged duration. These patients are exquisitely sensitive to nondepolarizing agents.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (8)

Bradycardia; bronchospasm; flushing; hypotension; muscle calcification (prolonged use); myopathy / acute quadriplegic syndrome (prolonged use); pruritus; skin rash. The "prolonged use" adverse reactions (calcification, myopathy) are ICU concerns for patients paralyzed for days — less relevant for transport but important to know. Bradycardia and hypotension are the most transport-relevant adverse reactions.
