🔬 Mechanism of Action

Benzodiazepine — its effects (anxiolytic, sedative, hypnotic, and skeletal muscle relaxant) are mediated by the inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA. Binds to the benzodiazepine site on GABA-A receptor → increases frequency of chloride channel opening → enhances GABA's inhibitory effect → CNS depression. Does NOT directly activate GABA — only enhances existing GABA activity (ceiling effect = safer than barbiturates).

💊 LORAZEPAM vs MIDAZOLAM — Benzo Comparison

Lorazepam (Ativan): Onset 1-2 min / peak 2 hr. Longer acting. No active metabolites (safer in hepatic impairment). q 45 min repeat for post-intub sedation. Preferred for sustained sedation/anxiolysis.

Midazolam (Versed): Onset 1-3 min / peak variable. Shorter acting. Has active metabolites. q 15 min repeat for post-intub sedation. Preferred for rapid-onset situations. Intranasal option (0.3 mg/kg IN).

🤰 Pregnancy

Lorazepam and its metabolite cross the placenta. Shorter-acting benzodiazepines are preferred in patients at risk for preterm delivery or when treatment is needed during the third trimester. However, benzos may be needed to treat maternal status epilepticus — treat when indicated.

⚡ SEIZURE CONTEXT — Fastest Repeat Interval

Lorazepam for seizures has the fastest repeat interval: q 5 min. Compare: combative = q 20 min, post-intub = q 45 min. This reflects the urgency of seizure management. Lorazepam 2-4 mg IV is a first-line anticonvulsant alongside midazolam and diazepam. Longer duration (6-8 hr) than midazolam provides sustained seizure suppression.

😴 POST-INTUBATION SEDATION CONTEXT (PCG 2.08)

Lorazepam is ONE of several post-intub sedation options per PCG 2.08: lorazepam 1-4 mg q 45 min, midazolam 2-5 mg q 15 min (or infusion), ketamine 1 mg/kg (or infusion), propofol infusion, dexmedetomidine infusion. Lorazepam's q 45 min interval means LONGER-acting but less titratable than midazolam or infusions. Best for stable patients who need sustained sedation with less frequent re-dosing.

🤢 REFRACTORY NAUSEA — Not First-Line

Lorazepam for nausea is a LAST-RESORT option. Per PCG 2.05 nausea treatment ladder: ondansetron first → promethazine → then lorazepam (if refractory to both). The 0.5-1 mg dose is the smallest lorazepam dose in the formulary. Benzodiazepine sedation is the mechanism for nausea relief, not a direct antiemetic effect.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (1)

Hypersensitivity. Simple CI per the LFN formulary.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (7)

Acute narrow-angle glaucoma; acute alcohol intoxication; renal or hepatic impairment; organic brain syndrome; myasthenia gravis; elderly and debilitated patients; limited pulmonary reserve.

• Glaucoma: Anticholinergic-like effects can worsen IOP
• Alcohol intoxication: Additive CNS depression → respiratory arrest
• Hepatic impairment: Lorazepam is actually safer than most benzos in hepatic disease (no active metabolites), but still use caution
• Myasthenia gravis: Muscle relaxant effect can worsen weakness
• Elderly/debilitated: More sensitive to CNS depression; reduce dose
• Limited pulmonary reserve: CNS depression → respiratory failure

🚫 Adverse Reactions (7)

Anterograde amnesia; drowsiness; sedation; dizziness; weakness; unsteadiness; hallucination; hypertension or hypotension.

Note: Both HTN and hypotension listed — BP effects are unpredictable. Anterograde amnesia is expected with all benzodiazepines — warn patients they may not remember the transport.
