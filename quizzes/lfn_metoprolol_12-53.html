🔬 Mechanism of Action

Selective inhibitor of beta₁-adrenergic receptors; competitively blocks beta₁-receptors.

Beta₁ receptor blockade in the heart reduces:
• Heart rate (SA node automaticity)
• Contractility (negative inotropy)
• Conduction velocity through the AV node
• Myocardial oxygen demand (rate-pressure product)

The selectivity for beta₁ (vs. beta₂ in lungs/peripheral vessels) makes it "cardioselective" — though selectivity is dose-dependent and diminishes at higher doses, which explains bronchospasm as an adverse reaction.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Metoprolol and the metabolite alpha-hydroxymetoprolol cross the placenta. Metoprolol may be used for the treatment of maternal ventricular arrhythmias, atrial fibrillation/atrial flutter, or supraventricular tachycardia during pregnancy. The LFN formulary explicitly supports its use in pregnancy for these arrhythmia indications.

🚫 SC #1 — AMIODARONE INTERACTION (Potentially Fatal)

Amiodarone may enhance the bradycardic effect of beta-blockers, possibly to the point of cardiac arrest. Amiodarone may increase the serum concentration of beta-blockers.

This is the most dangerous drug interaction in LFN 12.53. Both drugs slow conduction and reduce heart rate through different mechanisms. Amiodarone (class III antiarrhythmic) also has beta-blocking properties. The combination can produce:

• Severe refractory bradycardia
• Complete heart block
• Cardiac arrest

Additionally, amiodarone inhibits hepatic metabolism (CYP2D6), increasing metoprolol plasma levels. If a patient has already received amiodarone, approach metoprolol with extreme caution and have atropine and pacing immediately available.

⚠️ SC #2 — DIMINISHES DOBUTAMINE AND EPINEPHRINE

Beta-blockers may diminish the therapeutic effect of dobutamine and epinephrine.

This interaction has direct transport implications:
• A patient on metoprolol who receives epinephrine for anaphylaxis or cardiac arrest may have a blunted response — the beta-1 and beta-2 effects of epinephrine are partially blocked
• Higher doses of epinephrine may be needed
• Dobutamine's inotropic benefit is reduced in beta-blocked patients
• The alpha effects of epinephrine (vasoconstriction) are preserved since metoprolol doesn't block alpha receptors

⚠️ SC #3 — INCREASES LIDOCAINE CONCENTRATION

May increase the serum concentration of Lidocaine.

Metoprolol reduces hepatic blood flow (via reduced cardiac output), which can decrease hepatic clearance of lidocaine. Both drugs are used in arrhythmia management; if co-administered, monitor for lidocaine toxicity (CNS effects: seizures, confusion; cardiovascular: bradycardia, conduction delay). The interaction is pharmacokinetic, not pharmacodynamic — metoprolol isn't directly adding to lidocaine's effects, it's reducing lidocaine's clearance.

⚠️ SC #4 — ENHANCES HYPOTENSIVE EFFECTS

May enhance hypotensive effects of loop diuretics, nifedipine, and nitroprusside.

Any drug combination that lowers blood pressure through different mechanisms produces additive or synergistic hypotension. This is particularly relevant if a patient is already on these medications and receives IV metoprolol during transport. Watch for:
• Patient on furosemide (loop diuretic): already volume-depleted + reduced cardiac output from metoprolol = significant hypotension risk
• Patient on nifedipine (CCB): additive HR reduction and vasodilation
• Patient on nitroprusside: vasodilation + reduced CO = profound hypotension

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2 per LFN 12.53)

1. Hypersensitivity to Metoprolol, any component of the formulation, or other beta-blockers.
Cross-reactivity within the beta-blocker class means a prior allergic reaction to any beta-blocker is relevant.

2. Second- or third-degree heart block (except in patients with a functioning artificial pacemaker).
Metoprolol's AV node slowing in an already-blocked conduction system → complete block or asystole. The pacemaker exception is critical: if a ventricular demand pacemaker is functioning and capturing, the patient has a backup rhythm and metoprolol can be used with appropriate caution.

Note: First-degree AVB is NOT a listed contraindication — only 2nd and 3rd degree.

⚠️ Adverse Reactions

Bradycardia; hypotension; bronchospasm; CHF.

• Bradycardia: Direct result of SA/AV node beta₁ blockade — the primary therapeutic and adverse effect
• Hypotension: Reduced cardiac output from negative chronotropy and inotropy
• Bronchospasm: Loss of beta₁ selectivity at higher doses → beta₂ blockade in airways; greatest risk in asthma/COPD
• CHF: Reduced contractility can decompensate borderline cardiac function; acute IV use in low-EF patients carries significant risk

📋 CARDIAC ARREST USE — Refractory V-Fib

Per LFN 12.53: 5 mg IV, up to two total doses, in place of additional epinephrine.

This is a niche but important indication. In refractory ventricular fibrillation not responding to defibrillation + epinephrine, catecholamine excess (including from exogenous epinephrine) can perpetuate VF. Metoprolol can reduce sympathetic drive, potentially making the myocardium more amenable to defibrillation.
Key language: "in place of additional epinephrine" — this is a substitution, not an addition. When choosing metoprolol during refractory VF, you are using it instead of another epinephrine dose, not alongside it.
