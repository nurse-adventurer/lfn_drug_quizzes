🔬 Mechanism of Action

Slows conduction through the AV node, can interrupt reentry pathway through AV/SA nodes, and can restore normal sinus rhythm in patients with PSVT. The drug briefly "resets" the heart by temporarily blocking the AV node — stopping the reentry circuit that sustains SVT.

🤰 Pregnancy

Adenosine is an endogenous substance — adverse fetal effects would not be anticipated. Recommended for the acute treatment of SVT in pregnant women. One of the few LFN drugs with an explicitly favorable pregnancy recommendation.

  • Asystole may develop for up to 15 seconds following conversion. This is expected and transient. Warn the patient ("you may feel like your heart stops briefly") and be prepared — but don't panic during the pause.
  • Administer via rapid push, followed by a rapid flush of normal saline. Speed of administration is critical. A slow push allows the drug to be metabolized before reaching the heart. The NS flush chases the drug to the central circulation.
  • Administer in the most proximal IV/IO site as possible. Distal IV sites (hand, wrist) increase transit time → drug degrades before reaching the heart. Antecubital or above is ideal.
  • Decrease initial doses for cardiac transplant patients or if given via central venous access. Transplant hearts are denervated and hypersensitive to adenosine. Central lines deliver drug directly — no transit time degradation.
⚡ WHY SPEED MATTERS — Half-Life <10 Seconds

Adenosine's half-life is less than 10 seconds. It's rapidly metabolized by red blood cells and vascular endothelium. Every second between the syringe and the heart represents drug loss. This is why SC #2 (rapid push + flush) and SC #3 (proximal site) exist — they minimize transit time to maximize the amount of active drug reaching the AV node. A slow push through a hand IV = therapeutic failure.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (5)

Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome; atrial fibrillation; irregular wide complex tachycardia; sick sinus syndrome; 2nd & 3rd degree AV blocks.

🚫 WPW — The Critical Contraindication

In WPW, an accessory pathway bypasses the AV node. Blocking the AV node with adenosine forces ALL conduction through the accessory pathway — which has no rate-limiting capability. If atrial fibrillation is also present, this can result in ventricular fibrillation and death.

⚠️ Asthma — Bronchospasm Risk

Use cautiously in patients with asthma — adenosine may precipitate bronchospasm. Adenosine causes bronchoconstriction through mast cell activation and direct smooth muscle effects. Weigh SVT treatment benefit against bronchospasm risk.

🚫 Adverse Reactions

Cardiac arrest; ventricular arrhythmia; bradycardia; chest pain; nausea/vomiting. The cardiac arrest risk is primarily the expected post-conversion asystole (SC #1: up to 15 seconds). Ventricular arrhythmias are rare but possible.

📋 Why CI in Atrial Fibrillation?

Afib is an irregularly irregular rhythm — NOT a reentry SVT. Adenosine terminates SVT by breaking the reentry circuit. In afib, there is no reentry circuit to break. Adenosine would transiently slow the rate (AV block) but not convert it, while exposing the patient to unnecessary side effects.
