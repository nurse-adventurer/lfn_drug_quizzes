🔬 Mechanism of Action

Increases uterine contraction force and frequency.

As an ergot alkaloid, methylergonovine acts on uterine smooth muscle to produce stronger, more frequent contractions. The increased tone and contractility compresses the uterine blood vessels (spiral arteries), mechanically reducing hemorrhage.

This mechanism also explains the key systemic effect: ergot alkaloids cause vasoconstriction throughout the body via alpha-adrenergic and serotonin receptor activity — which is why elevated SBP is a hard contraindication.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Methylergonovine is used for the treatment of postpartum hemorrhage due to uterine atony. Methylergonovine is recommended when a supplemental uterotonic agent is needed; due to maternal side effects it is not preferred for initial use. This confirms its role as a second-line agent — not first-line — in PPH management.

🚫 SBP > 140 — THE CRITICAL DOSING RULE

The LFN formulary explicitly states: "Do not administer if SBP > 140."

This is embedded directly in the dosing line — not just in the CI list. It is the most operationally critical fact about methylergonovine for flight nurses. Before every dose:

1. Obtain a blood pressure
2. Check SBP against the 140 threshold
3. If SBP ≤ 140 → proceed
4. If SBP > 140 → hold and contact medical direction

This threshold catches: preeclampsia, eclampsia, chronic hypertension, and any stress-related BP elevation in the postpartum patient.

⚠️ SUPPLEMENTAL / SECOND-LINE ROLE — Not Preferred for Initial Use

Per LFN 12.51 Pregnancy Considerations: methylergonovine is recommended when a supplemental uterotonic agent is needed; due to maternal side effects it is not preferred for initial use.

In the clinical PPH sequence, oxytocin remains the preferred first-line uterotonic. Methylergonovine steps in when oxytocin alone is insufficient. The "maternal side effects" referenced include the systemic vasoconstrictive adverse reactions (hypertension, cerebrovascular accident risk) and the significant GI/metabolic adverse reaction profile.

📋 PHARMACOKINETIC PROFILE — Immediately / 15 min / 45 min

Onset: Immediately. Peak: 15 minutes. Duration: 45 minutes.

The immediate onset means uterine contraction begins essentially as soon as the drug reaches the myometrium via IM absorption. The relatively short 45-minute duration explains the q 2-4 hour repeat dosing interval — the drug is well off its peak and largely cleared before the next dose is appropriate. In transport, this timing is operationally useful: if the first dose doesn't significantly improve tone within 15–20 minutes, the drug is at peak effect and the response is likely the best you'll get without dose adjustment or agent change.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (3)

SBP > 140; hypersensitivity; intra- or periarterial administration.

• SBP > 140: Ergot alkaloid vasoconstriction on top of elevated BP → hypertensive crisis, stroke, MI
• Hypersensitivity: Standard allergy contraindication
• Intra- or periarterial: Direct arterial injection causes intense local vasospasm → ischemia, tissue necrosis. Ensure you are in a patent venous site. Confirm IM injection is truly intramuscular.

⚠️ Warning / Precaution

Not for routine IV administration due to risk of inducing sudden hypertensive and cerebrovascular accidents.

IV administration can precipitate a sudden, severe hypertensive crisis with potential for stroke. The IM route allows for controlled absorption. If IV is ever considered in extraordinary circumstances, this requires direct medical direction authorization — it is not a standing order route.

🚫 Adverse Reactions — Primarily GI/Metabolic

Hypokalemia (severe); metabolic alkalosis (severe); seizures; intestinal obstruction; intestinal necrosis; GI bleeding; ischemic colitis; GI perforation; gastric irritation; anorexia; nausea; vomiting; constipation; hypocalcemia; hypomagnesemia; sodium retention; diarrhea; fecal impaction.

This is predominantly a GI and electrolyte adverse effect profile — not purely cardiovascular as one might expect from a vasoconstrictive agent. The severe hypokalemia and metabolic alkalosis are the most clinically significant for transport monitoring. Intestinal ischemia/necrosis and GI perforation, while rare, reflect the degree of vasoconstriction this drug class can induce in mesenteric vessels.
