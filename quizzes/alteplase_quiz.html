🔬 Mechanism of Action

Initiates fibrinolysis by binding to fibrin in a thrombus and converting entrapped plasminogen to plasmin (the active substance), which works to break up blood clots. tPA is "clot-selective" — it preferentially activates plasminogen bound to fibrin within the clot, providing some localized action. However, systemic plasminogen activation still occurs, creating significant bleeding risk.

🤰 Pregnancy

Based on molecular weight, alteplase is not expected to cross the placenta. However, the bleeding risk is the primary concern in pregnancy. Use only when benefit clearly outweighs risk — massive stroke with significant disability potential.

  • Monitor for signs of bleeding or anaphylaxis. Continuous neuro checks, vital signs q 15 min during infusion, watch for: new headache, vomiting, neurological deterioration (suggests ICH), bleeding from IV sites, gum/nasal bleeding, hematuria.
  • Verify with sending RN that excess medication was removed from bottle per manufacturer instructions. The vial contains 100 mg but most patients receive less. Failure to remove excess = potential overdose = increased hemorrhage risk. This is a critical safety verification.
🔍 EXCESS MEDICATION REMOVAL — Why This Matters

Standard tPA vial = 100 mg reconstituted to 100 mL (1 mg/mL). A 70 kg patient receives 63 mg total, meaning 37 mg must be removed before starting infusion. If the sending facility fails to remove the excess, the patient receives 100 mg instead of 63 mg — a 59% overdose that dramatically increases hemorrhage risk. Always verify this step during handoff.

⏱️ POST-INFUSION — The NS Flush

Once infusion is complete: infuse 100 mL of NS through the IV tubing at the same rate of infusion. IV tubing dead space holds 10–20 mL of fluid. At 1 mg/mL concentration, this could be 10–20 mg of tPA remaining in tubing. The NS flush ensures complete drug delivery without causing a bolus effect.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Active internal bleeding; history of CVA; intracranial hemorrhage (within last 3 months); intracranial or intraspinal surgery or trauma. Any of these = absolute stop. Do NOT administer tPA.

🚫 Warnings / Precautions (3 Critical Rules)

1. High risk of internal bleeding. tPA activates plasmin systemically, not just at the clot. Every vessel in the body is at risk.
2. Do not give if patient has abnormal CT scan. CT required to rule out hemorrhagic stroke — giving tPA to a hemorrhagic stroke = catastrophic.
3. Should only be given in discussion with a receiving neurologist. This is not a field crew decision — neurologist consultation is mandatory.

⚠️ Adverse Reactions (3)

Bleeding; anaphylaxis; angioedema. Bleeding is the most common and most dangerous. Angioedema (orolingual swelling) occurs in 1–5% of patients — risk increased with ACE inhibitor use. Can compromise airway.

🩸 Hemorrhagic Conversion — The Feared Complication

Signs during transport: new/worsening headache, vomiting, sudden neurological deterioration, declining GCS, new pupil asymmetry, seizure. If suspected: STOP the infusion immediately, obtain stat CT if possible, prepare for emergency airway management and ICP treatment. Notify receiving facility.
