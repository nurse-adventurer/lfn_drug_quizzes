🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Stimulates contraction of uterine smooth muscle by increasing intracellular calcium concentrations thus mimicking contraction of normal, spontaneous labor and transiently impeding uterine blood flow."

Receptor Binding
Binds oxytocin receptors on myometrium → phospholipase C activation
↑ Intracellular Ca²⁺
Calcium release from SR + influx → myosin light chain kinase activated
Contraction
Actin-myosin cross-bridging → uterine smooth muscle contracts → mimics labor
Hemostasis
Contracted myometrium mechanically compresses open spiral arteries at placental site → stops bleeding
Uterine Blood Flow
"Transiently impedes uterine blood flow" — same compression mechanism; relevant to fetal circulation

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

"Small amounts of exogenous oxytocin are expected to reach the fetal circulation. When used as indicated, teratogenic effects would not be expected. Nonteratogenic adverse reactions are reported in the neonate as well as the mother."

Used postpartum/peripartum — organogenesis is complete, so teratogenicity is not the concern. The neonatal adverse reaction note acknowledges that uterine hyperstimulation can cause fetal hypoxia — a nonteratogenic but clinically significant effect.

💊 Why Two Concentrations? — The Clinical Logic

Tier 1 (routine postpartum/delivery) uses 20 units/1000mL with a "wide open" initial bolus — the goal is uterotonic prophylaxis and facilitating placental delivery in a hemodynamically stable patient.

Tier 2 (PPH/uterine inversion) doubles the concentration to 40 units/1000mL but specifies a 500mL over 10 minutes load rather than "wide open." This controlled rapid infusion delivers oxytocin urgently while avoiding the cardiovascular effects of too-rapid delivery (vasodilation, hypotension, reflex tachycardia). The maintenance rate halves to 50mL/hr — a more concentrated solution at a slower rate delivers similar oxytocin per hour while reducing total fluid volume in a patient who may be hemodynamically compromised.

⏱️ Duration: 20 Minutes After Discontinuation

Oxytocin's duration is 20 minutes after d/c. The uterotonic effect persists approximately 20 minutes after the infusion stops, then dissipates.

Infusion gap risk: If the bag runs dry, tubing disconnects, or the infusion is accidentally stopped, uterine tone will be lost within ~20 minutes. Rebleed risk rises after this window — making continuous infusion maintenance a transport priority, not a set-and-forget task.

Adverse effect persistence: If oxytocin causes hypertension, stopping the infusion will not provide immediate relief — the effect will persist for ~20 more minutes. This intersects directly with the vasopressor interaction warning.

💉 IM Route: Tier 2 Only — and Why

The 10 units IM backup is listed exclusively under Tier 2 indications (PPH, uterine inversion). It is not listed for Tier 1 routine delivery management.

This reflects clinical priority — IM administration is an emergency bridge for life-threatening PPH when IV access has not yet been established. The correct field sequence: give 10 units IM immediately while simultaneously establishing IV access, then transition to the full Tier 2 infusion protocol. IM onset is slower than IV infusion but avoids cardiovascular risks of rapid IV delivery and provides initial uterotonic support during the critical window.

⚠️ VASOPRESSOR INTERACTION — The Transport Scenario
LFN 12.66 Warning: "Vasopressors may potentiate hypertension."

Oxytocin itself lists HTN as an adverse reaction. Adding any vasopressor (norepinephrine 12.63, epinephrine 12.26, vasopressin 12.83, dopamine) creates additive pressor effects.

Real transport scenario: A patient with PPH-induced hemorrhagic shock may simultaneously receive oxytocin (uterotonic) + norepinephrine (hemodynamic support). This combination requires BP monitoring and careful vasopressor titration. The patient with preeclampsia or gestational hypertension faces even higher risk from this additive effect.

Compare: Methylergonovine (12.51) has SBP >140 as an explicit contraindication — reflecting even stronger vasoconstrictor properties. Oxytocin lists this only as a warning, but the risk vector is identical.
🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (4)
Fetal Distress Where Delivery Not Imminent
Oxytocin → ↑ contractions → ↓ uteroplacental blood flow → worsens fetal hypoxia. If delivery cannot be quickly accomplished, CI applies.
Significant Cephalopelvic Disproportion
Fetal head cannot pass through pelvis → forcing labor with oxytocin creates uterine rupture risk without delivery benefit.
Hypertonic Uterus
Already contracting too frequently/forcefully (tachysystole). Adding oxytocin → hyperstimulation → fetal hypoxia + uterine rupture risk.
Where Vaginal Delivery Is Contraindicated
Covers placenta previa, vasa previa, prior classical C-section, cord presentation, transverse lie — any indication for cesarean delivery.

Pattern: All 4 CIs are mechanical/obstetric in nature — they reflect situations where uterine contraction causes harm rather than benefit. There is no hemodynamic CI (compare: methylergonovine's SBP >140 CI).

⚠️ Warning (1)

Vasopressors may potentiate hypertension.

Single warning. Clinically relevant in the PPH-with-shock scenario where vasopressors are co-administered. Monitor BP; titrate vasopressor conservatively when oxytocin is running.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (4)
Nausea Vomiting Uterine Rupture HTN

Uterine rupture is the highest-acuity adverse reaction — overstimulation in a scarred uterus (prior C-section, myomectomy) is highest risk. Signs: sudden contraction cessation, severe abdominal pain, loss of fetal station, hemodynamic collapse. Requires emergent surgical intervention.

HTN is the mechanistic basis for the vasopressor warning and is clinically relevant in patients with underlying hypertensive disorders of pregnancy (preeclampsia, gestational HTN).
