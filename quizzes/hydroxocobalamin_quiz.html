💊 NOTE THE DOSE UNITS — This is a GRAM-Dose Drug

5 GRAMS — not milligrams. Hydroxocobalamin is one of the few LFN drugs dosed in grams. The large dose is required because it binds cyanide in approximately a 1:1 molar ratio — you need enough hydroxocobalamin molecules to bind ALL circulating cyanide. The Cyanokit comes as a lyophilized powder that is reconstituted with 200 mL of NS to create the 5 g dose. This entire 200 mL volume is infused over 15 minutes.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Binds cyanide to form nontoxic cyanocobalamin, which is then excreted in urine. This is one of the most elegant mechanisms in pharmacology: hydroxocobalamin (a form of Vitamin B12 precursor) has a cobalt center that has extremely high affinity for cyanide (CN⁻). It binds cyanide directly, forming cyanocobalamin (which IS Vitamin B12). The resulting B12 is nontoxic and is excreted by the kidneys. The drug literally converts a lethal poison into a vitamin.

☠️ CYANIDE POISONING — Recognition in Smoke Inhalation

Cyanide poisoning most commonly occurs in the LFN transport context from smoke inhalation — burning plastics, synthetics, and household materials release hydrogen cyanide (HCN). Cyanide blocks cytochrome oxidase in mitochondria → cells cannot use oxygen → cellular hypoxia despite adequate O₂ delivery.

Classic presentation clues:
• Fire/smoke exposure (especially enclosed space)
• Altered mental status / coma (GCS depression)
• Normal or high SpO₂ (cells can't USE oxygen, but it's in the blood)
• Severe metabolic acidosis (lactic acidosis from anaerobic metabolism)
• Seizures, cardiovascular collapse in severe cases
• Cherry-red skin (late, unreliable sign)

🤰 Pregnancy

Hydroxocobalamin crosses the placenta. Data are limited. However: "Medications used as antidotes should take into consideration the health and prognosis of the mother; antidotes should be administered to pregnant females if there is a clear indication for use and should not be withheld because of fears of teratogenicity." Cyanide poisoning is immediately life-threatening to both mother and fetus — the antidote should not be withheld.

🔴 SC #1 — RED DISCOLORATION: Expected, NOT an Adverse Reaction

May cause reddish coloring of skin or urine. Hydroxocobalamin is an intensely dark red compound (it's a cobalt-containing molecule related to B12). After administration, the red color distributes throughout body fluids causing: red/orange skin discoloration (can last days), red/brown urine (can persist for weeks), and interference with colorimetric lab tests (falsely elevated bilirubin, falsely decreased creatinine). This is NOT hemolysis, NOT an allergic reaction, and NOT a sign of toxicity. Warn the patient and receiving team.

💉 DEDICATED IV LINE — Critical Administration Requirement

Per Warnings: Multiple drug interactions, should be given in dedicated IV/IO line. Hydroxocobalamin's cobalt center and deep red color can interact with and precipitate other IV medications. Never run it through the same line as other drugs. If the patient has only one IV, either start a second line or flush thoroughly between medications. In cardiac arrest with cyanide poisoning, this creates a logistics challenge — you need a dedicated line for the antidote AND separate access for ACLS drugs.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (1)

Hypersensitivity. Single, simple CI. Given that cyanide poisoning is rapidly fatal, the threshold to give hydroxocobalamin is very low — even in patients with possible hypersensitivity, the life-saving benefit usually outweighs the allergy risk in true cyanide toxicity.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (1)

Multiple drug interactions, should be given in dedicated IV/IO line. The cobalt center in hydroxocobalamin interacts with numerous other medications. This is both a drug interaction warning AND an administration instruction. Always use a dedicated line.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (4)

Hypertension; hypokalemia; renal injury; thrombocytosis.

• Hypertension: Most common — possibly from nitric oxide (NO) scavenging by the cobalt center. Usually transient and self-limiting. Significant because many cyanide patients may already be hypotensive.
• Hypokalemia: Mechanism unclear; monitor electrolytes.
• Renal injury: Red/crystallized hydroxocobalamin in renal tubules; usually reversible.
• Thrombocytosis: Elevated platelet count; mechanism unclear; usually transient.

Note: red discoloration (SC #1) is NOT listed as an adverse reaction — it's a predictable cosmetic effect, not a harmful one.
