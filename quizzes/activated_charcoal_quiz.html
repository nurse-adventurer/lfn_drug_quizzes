🔬 Mechanism of Action

ADsorbs (not ABsorbs) toxic substances, thus inhibiting GI absorption and preventing or limiting systemic toxicity. Multiple doses may also interrupt enteroenteric, enterohepatic, and enterogastric circulation of some drugs; may also adsorb any unabsorbed drug remaining in the GI tract.

🤰 Pregnancy

Use during pregnancy is not expected to result in significant exposure to the fetus. Charcoal works entirely within the GI tract and is not systemically absorbed. Safe to use in pregnancy when indicated for toxic ingestion.

  • Most effective if administered within 30–60 minutes of ingestion. Efficacy drops dramatically after this window as drugs are absorbed into systemic circulation. However, delayed administration may still have some benefit for sustained-release formulations or drugs that slow GI motility.
  • Ineffective for iron, lithium, heavy metals, and other ions. These are small ionic/charged compounds that don't adsorb to charcoal's carbon surface. No amount of charcoal will bind them.
  • Milk products ingested prior to administration can reduce effectiveness. Milk proteins and fats coat charcoal particles, blocking adsorption sites. Always ask: "Did anyone give the patient anything to drink?"
  • May reduce effectiveness of other antidotes such as acetylcysteine. Oral NAC (Mucomyst) for acetaminophen OD can be adsorbed by charcoal in the gut. Communicate charcoal timing to receiving facility — they may choose IV NAC instead.

🔄 CHARCOAL + NAC INTERACTION (SC #4)

If charcoal was given for acetaminophen OD, oral N-acetylcysteine (NAC/Mucomyst) may be ~40% less effective because charcoal adsorbs it. Solutions: (1) separate timing, (2) increase oral NAC dose, (3) use IV NAC (Acetadote) which bypasses the GI tract entirely. Always communicate charcoal administration time to the receiving toxicology team.

📋 CONTRAINDICATIONS

There are no absolute contraindications listed within the manufacturer's labeling. This is unusual — most drugs have at least hypersensitivity. However, the warnings below function as practical contraindications in specific scenarios.

🚫 AVOID — Hydrocarbon & Caustic Ingestions

Avoid use in hydrocarbon and caustic ingestions. Charcoal doesn't adsorb these substances AND can cause harm: may induce vomiting (re-exposure to caustic tissue damage), obscures endoscopy, and NG placement risks perforating damaged esophagus.

🚫 INEFFECTIVE — Low Molecular Weight Compounds

Not effective for cyanide, iron, ethanol, methanol, or lithium. These are small, charged, or rapidly absorbed molecules that don't bind to charcoal's carbon surface. Mnemonic: "CHILE" — Cyanide, Heavy metals, Iron, Lithium, Ethanol/alcohols.

⚠️ Vomiting Risk — Especially with Sorbitol

Charcoal may cause vomiting; risk is greater when administered with sorbitol. Vomiting + altered mental status = aspiration risk. Charcoal aspiration causes severe chemical pneumonitis. Position laterally, have suction ready.

⚠️ Decreased Peristalsis — Use with Caution

Use with caution in patients with decreased peristalsis. Risk of intestinal obstruction, prolonged charcoal retention, and aspiration. Consider: gastroparesis, anticholinergic overdoses (slowed gut), opioid co-ingestion.

🚫 Adverse Reactions

GI: Abdominal distention, appendicitis, constipation, dental/mouth discoloration (black, temporary), fecal discoloration (black), intestinal obstruction, vomiting.
Respiratory: Aspiration, respiratory failure. Aspiration is the most feared complication — charcoal in the lungs causes severe chemical pneumonitis.
