✅ LFN AND 2025 ACLS ALIGNED — 1 mg for Bradycardia

Both LFN protocol and the 2025 ACLS guidelines use 1 mg IV for adult symptomatic bradycardia. Prior ACLS editions used 0.5 mg — if you see 0.5 mg on older reference cards or in older training materials, it is outdated. The current standard is 1 mg.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Competitive antagonist that selectively blocks all muscarinic responses to acetylcholine (ACh). Blocks vagal impulses to the heart, which increases SA node discharge. Suppresses sweating, lacrimation, salivation, and secretions from the upper and lower respiratory tract.

🤰 Pregnancy

Antidotes should be administered to pregnant women if there is a clear indication and should not be withheld because of fears of teratogenicity. Medications for cardiac arrest in pregnancy follow standard ACLS guidelines. Appropriate medications should not be withheld.

📋 No Numbered Special Considerations in LFN 12.09

Unlike many LFN formulary entries, atropine 12.09 has no numbered SCs. However, the Warnings section contains critical clinical considerations that function similarly. Key clinical application notes:

☠️ ORGANOPHOSPHATE — Endpoint is SECRETIONS, Not Heart Rate

In organophosphate poisoning, give 2–5 mg IV/IM, repeat q 5 min until secretions have decreased. The endpoint is DRY secretions, NOT heart rate. Tachycardia is acceptable — keep giving atropine until the lungs are clear and secretions stop. Severe cases may require 10–100+ mg. There is NO maximum dose for organophosphate treatment. Also give pralidoxime (2-PAM) per LFN 12.70 to reactivate acetylcholinesterase.

👶 RSI PRETREATMENT — Only Neonates (≤28 days)

RSI pretreatment with atropine is only for patients ≤28 days old (neonates). Dose: 0.02 mg/kg IV. No adult dose listed (N/A). This prevents vagal-mediated bradycardia during laryngoscopy in neonates, who are highly dependent on heart rate for cardiac output. Per PCG 8.27, the RSI protocol also notes atropine for bradycardia during intubation attempts: adult 1 mg IV q 3–5 min, peds 0.02 mg/kg IV.

💊 BB/CCB OVERDOSE — Different Max Than Bradycardia

For beta-blocker/calcium channel blocker overdose: 1 mg IV, repeat q 3 min, max total 0.04 mg/kg. Note the max is weight-based (0.04 mg/kg) vs the flat 3 mg max for bradycardia. For a 70 kg patient: 0.04 × 70 = 2.8 mg max. Peds: No LFN Standing Order for BB/CCB OD. Atropine is often ineffective in severe BB/CCB OD — have calcium gluconate, glucagon, and high-dose insulin ready.

📋 Contraindications

NONE. Atropine has NO contraindications per LFN 12.09. This is unusual — most drugs have at least hypersensitivity listed. The absence of CIs reflects atropine's critical role as a life-saving antidote in multiple emergencies where withholding it could be fatal.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (3)

1. Likely ineffective in type II and III heart blocks and cardiac transplant patients. Type II (Mobitz II) and Type III (complete) heart blocks are infranodal — below the AV node where atropine acts. Transplant hearts are denervated (no vagal innervation to block).
2. May increase ventricular rates in atrial fibrillation/flutter. By speeding AV conduction, more atrial impulses reach the ventricles → faster ventricular rate.
3. May raise intraocular pressure in patients with glaucoma. Mydriasis blocks aqueous outflow in narrow-angle glaucoma.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (6)

Pupil dilation; headache; dizziness; hypertension; paradoxical bradycardia; eye dryness. "Paradoxical bradycardia" is the most tested: inadequate doses (below therapeutic threshold) can stimulate central vagal centers, INCREASING vagal outflow and actually SLOWING the heart. This is why minimum doses exist (0.1 mg peds).

⚠️ Anticholinergic Toxidrome — "Hot, Dry, Red, Mad, Blind"

At therapeutic and supratherapeutic doses: Blind as a bat (mydriasis, blurred vision), Dry as a bone (↓ secretions), Red as a beet (cutaneous vasodilation), Mad as a hatter (confusion, agitation), Hot as a hare (can't sweat → hyperthermia). These are expected effects, not allergic reactions. Self-limiting as drug wears off.
