🔬 Mechanism of Action

Suppresses acute and chronic inflammation. In addition, it potentiates vascular smooth muscle relaxation by beta-adrenergic agonists and may alter airway hyperactivity. In croup, dexamethasone reduces subglottic edema through anti-inflammatory effects: inhibits phospholipase A2 (reducing prostaglandin/leukotriene synthesis), decreases capillary permeability, stabilizes lysosomal membranes, and suppresses inflammatory cytokines. The beta-agonist potentiation enhances racemic epinephrine's bronchodilatory effect when both are used.

⏰ WHY ~72 HOURS MATTERS — Single Dose Covers the Course

Croup symptoms typically peak over 2–3 days then resolve. Dexamethasone's ~72-hour duration means a single dose covers the entire symptom course. This is the primary advantage over methylprednisolone (~18–36 hr) or prednisone (12–36 hr), which would need repeat dosing. Single-dose treatment improves compliance and reduces ED revisits.

🤰 Pregnancy

Dexamethasone crosses the placenta and is partially metabolized by placental enzymes to an inactive metabolite. Note: dexamethasone 12.17 is a peds-only indication (croup), so the pregnancy section is primarily informational. For antenatal corticosteroid use (fetal lung maturation), see betamethasone 12.10.

📋 No Numbered Special Considerations in LFN 12.17

Dexamethasone has no SCs listed. The key clinical context is built into the dosing table notation: "Preferred if available, otherwise use methylprednisolone."

👶 CROUP — Clinical Recognition

Classic presentation: 6 months to 3 years (peak age), preceded by URI symptoms, worse at night, "barking" or "seal-like" cough, inspiratory stridor, hoarseness. Caused by parainfluenza virus (most common). The pediatric subglottic airway is especially vulnerable — 1 mm of circumferential edema reduces cross-sectional area by ~44% in infants (vs ~25% in adults).

🩺 SEVERITY ASSESSMENT — Guides Treatment Intensity

Mild: Barking cough, no stridor at rest, no retractions → Dexamethasone alone
Moderate: Stridor at rest, mild retractions → Dexamethasone + consider racemic epi
Severe: Marked stridor at rest, significant retractions, agitation, hypoxia → Dexamethasone + racemic epinephrine (0.5 mL of 2.25% in 2.5 mL NS per LFN 12.73) + O₂ + prepare for advanced airway
All severities receive dexamethasone. Racemic epi added for moderate-severe.

⚠️ VOMITED ORAL DOSE — Switch Routes

If the child vomits oral dexamethasone within 15–20 minutes, assume minimal absorption. Re-dose via IM or IV at the same dose (0.6 mg/kg, max 12 mg). Dexamethasone has excellent bioavailability across all routes (oral ~80%, IM/IV ~100%). Don't re-attempt oral if the child already vomited once.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity to corticosteroids. True corticosteroid allergy is rare but possible. If the child has a documented reaction to any corticosteroid, do not give dexamethasone or methylprednisolone.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

May cause hypertension and hyperglycemia. Single-dose dexamethasone for croup rarely causes clinically significant hypertension or hyperglycemia in otherwise healthy children. In diabetic children, monitor glucose closely — steroid-induced hyperglycemia can be significant and may require insulin adjustment for 24–48 hours given the ~72-hour duration.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (4)

Nausea; vomiting; headache; dizziness. These are generally mild and transient with single-dose therapy. Behavioral changes (hyperactivity, irritability, sleep disturbance) are steroid class effects that may occur but aren't specifically listed in LFN 12.17.
