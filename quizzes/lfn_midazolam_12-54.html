🔬 Mechanism of Action

Midazolam is a short-acting parenteral benzodiazepine with CNS depressant, muscle relaxant, anticonvulsant, and anterograde amnestic effects.

Intensifies activity of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), a major inhibitory neurotransmitter of the brain, by interfering with its reuptake and promoting its accumulation at neuronal synapses.

Clinical effects:
• Sedation / anxiolysis
• Anterograde amnesia (patient won't remember peri-procedure period)
• Muscle relaxation
• Anticonvulsant
• Does NOT provide analgesia — pain must be managed separately

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Midazolam crosses the placenta. Data related to the treatment of status epilepticus in pregnancy are limited; use of midazolam may be considered. When a pregnant patient has a seizure requiring intervention, the maternal benefit of seizure control outweighs the risk of placental transfer.

📋 ADULT INFUSION PROTOCOL — Weight-Based Starting Rate

Step 1: Give bolus dose (2-5 mg IV for sedation, or indication-appropriate dose)
Step 2: Start infusion based on weight:
• <100 kg → Start at 2 mg/hr
• ≥100 kg → Start at 4 mg/hr

Step 3: Titrate by 1 mg/hr q 5-10 min to effect
Maximum: 10 mg/hr

Between titrations: Give additional 2 mg bolus before each rate increase — this bridges the gap while the new infusion rate reaches steady state.

📋 PEDIATRIC INFUSION PROTOCOL — Weight-Based mcg/kg/hr

Step 1: Give bolus dose (indication-appropriate)
Step 2: Start infusion at 50 mcg/kg/hr
Step 3: Titrate by 10 mcg/kg/hr q 5-10 min
Maximum: 100 mcg/kg/hr

Before each titration: Give additional bolus prior to increasing rate

Note: Peds infusion uses mcg/kg/hr (micrograms), not mg/hr. Convert for pump programming: 50 mcg/kg/hr = 0.05 mg/kg/hr.

⚠️ When to Consider Infusion

LFN 12.54 specifies "consider infusion for long transports" under both sedation (combative) and post-intubation sedation indications. Bolus dosing every 15 minutes may be sufficient for short transports; infusions provide more consistent serum levels and reduce the peaks-and-troughs of intermittent bolusing on extended transports.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATION — Hypersensitivity Only

Known hypersensitivity to midazolam or its components is the only listed contraindication in LFN 12.54.

Important distinction: Acute narrow-angle glaucoma is NOT a contraindication for midazolam per LFN 12.54 — that CI belongs to diazepam (LFN 12.20). This is a common point of confusion because both are benzodiazepines. Always verify CIs by drug, not by drug class.

The LFN 12.54 CI list is minimal: hypersensitivity only. Clinical cautions (elderly, opioid co-administration, alcohol intoxication) are listed as Warnings, not CIs.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (2 per LFN 12.54)

1. Alcohol intoxication
Alcohol potentiates CNS and respiratory depression through GABA enhancement. In an acutely intoxicated patient, midazolam doses produce substantially more sedation and respiratory depression than in a sober patient. The synergy is additive to supraadditive — clinical effect can far exceed what dose calculations predict.

2. Concomitant use of opioids
Opioids (fentanyl, morphine) and benzodiazepines together produce respiratory depression that is greater than either drug alone. This is one of the most common causes of iatrogenic respiratory compromise in transport. When both are used together (e.g., post-intubation sedation with fentanyl + midazolam), monitor ventilation continuously. LFN 12.30 (Fentanyl) also requires ETCO2 monitoring with multiple doses or sedative combinations.

🚫 Adverse Reactions

Hypotension; nausea/vomiting; respiratory depression.

• Hypotension: Midazolam reduces sympathetic tone and has mild vasodilatory effects. Most significant in volume-depleted, elderly, or catecholamine-dependent patients. RSI dosing in hypotensive patients uses the reduced 0.1 mg/kg dose for this reason.
• Respiratory depression: The most clinically dangerous adverse effect — dose-dependent, worsened by opioid co-administration and alcohol. Have BVM and reversal agent (flumazenil 12.31) immediately available.
• Nausea/vomiting: Less common but relevant post-sedation, particularly with IN administration.

⚠️ Key Points

  • Section 12.54 — not 12.53
  • CI: Hypersensitivity only — narrow-angle glaucoma is diazepam's CI (12.20), NOT midazolam's
  • Warnings: Alcohol intoxication; concomitant opioid use (both ↑ respiratory depression)
  • IN dose is always 0.3 mg/kg for ALL indications — split between both nostrils, max 10 mg
  • Seizure repeat q5 min; Sedation/post-intubation repeat q15 min; Anxiolysis q15 min
  • RSI: 0.2 mg/kg (specific dose, not range); 0.1 mg/kg if hypotensive; max 20 mg; wait 3 min before paralytic
  • Infusion adult: <100 kg = 2 mg/hr start; ≥100 kg = 4 mg/hr start; titrate 1 mg/hr; max 10 mg/hr; give 2 mg bolus between titrations
  • Peds post-intubation: Max single dose 2.5 mg (lower ceiling than adult)
  • Anxiolysis dose (0.01 mg/kg) is much lower than sedation dose (2-5 mg) — do not confuse
  • Onset/Peak/Duration: 1-2 min / 3-5 min / 30 min | Mixture: 10 mg / 100 mL
  • Adverse reactions: Hypotension, nausea/vomiting, respiratory depression
  • Reversal: Flumazenil 12.31 (0.2 mg IV q45 sec; max 1 mg adult)
