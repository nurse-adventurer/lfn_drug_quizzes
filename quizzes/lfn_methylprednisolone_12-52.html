🔬 Mechanism of Action

Corticosteroids exert a wide array of physiologic effects including modulation of carbohydrate, protein, and lipid metabolism and maintenance of fluid and electrolyte homeostasis.

Cardiovascular, immunologic, musculoskeletal, endocrine, and neurologic physiologies are all influenced.

Primary anti-inflammatory mechanisms:
• Suppression of migration of polymorphonuclear leukocytes
• Reversal of increased capillary permeability

This explains the delayed onset (1-6 hours) — these are gene-transcription-mediated effects, not immediate receptor activation. Peak effect at 8 hours; duration 18-36 hours.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Methylprednisolone crosses the placenta. Some products contain benzyl alcohol, which may also cross the placenta. Inhaled corticosteroids are recommended for treatment of asthma during pregnancy; however, systemic corticosteroids should be used to control acute exacerbations. The LFN indications (asthma, allergic reaction) that may arise in pregnancy are still appropriate for systemic methylprednisolone when clinically indicated.

📋 TWO-TIER INDICATION STRUCTURE — Know the Difference

Tier 1 — LFN Standing Order (adult + peds):
Allergic reaction / Asthma / COPD / Croup / High Altitude Emergencies / Allergic Reactions
→ Adult: 125 mg IV  |  Peds: 2 mg/kg IV (max 60 mg)

Tier 2 — Provider Order Required (adult only):
Refractory hypotension in septic shock
→ Adult: 125 mg IV, only if hydrocortisone is unavailable
→ Peds: No LFN Standing Order

The septic shock tier exists because hydrocortisone has mineralocorticoid activity that is beneficial in vasopressor-refractory septic shock — methylprednisolone is a fallback only when hydrocortisone cannot be obtained.

⚠️ CROUP — Dexamethasone Is Preferred

Per LFN 12.17, dexamethasone is the preferred agent for croup if available. Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV / 2 mg/kg peds is the fallback when dexamethasone is unavailable.

Dexamethasone advantages for croup: longer duration (~72 hours vs. 18-36 hours), can be given IM/PO, well-established in the croup literature. Methylprednisolone covers the indication adequately but is not the first choice.

📋 ONSET/PEAK/DURATION — Why Timing Matters

Onset: 1-6 hours | Peak: 8 hours | Duration: 18-36 hours

The 1-6 hour onset is critical for flight: methylprednisolone given during transport will NOT produce significant anti-inflammatory effect before arrival at a receiving facility in most transport scenarios. It is being given to establish the therapeutic trajectory — so that the drug is working at the 4-8 hour mark after administration.

This is why bronchodilators (albuterol, ipratropium) are the acute symptom-relief agents, and methylprednisolone is given concurrently to reduce the inflammatory component over the following hours.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATION

Known hypersensitivity.

This is the only LFN-listed contraindication for methylprednisolone. Unlike many other drugs with long CI lists, the corticosteroid's broad physiologic utility means the only hard stop is known allergy to the drug or its formulation components. This makes it one of the most broadly applicable agents in the LFN formulary — the indication and provider order status are the primary gatekeepers, not contraindications.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (2)

1. May cause hyperglycemia in diabetic patients.
Corticosteroids stimulate hepatic gluconeogenesis and reduce peripheral glucose uptake. Diabetic patients receiving methylprednisolone can experience significant glucose elevation — monitor blood glucose and treat with insulin as needed.

2. May cause hypercortisolism or suppression of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, particularly in younger children.
Single-dose acute use in transport is unlikely to cause clinically significant HPA suppression, but it is a relevant concern in younger children who have smaller baseline cortisol reserves. This is also why repeated dosing or prolonged courses carry a greater warning burden.

🚫 Adverse Reactions — Cardiovascular-Focused per LFN

Bradycardia; cardiac arrhythmia; cardiomegaly; circulatory shock; edema; hiccups; nausea/vomiting.

The LFN adverse reaction list is notably cardiovascular-dominant — not the general steroid side-effect profile that includes infection, osteoporosis, or psychiatric effects. In the acute transport context:
• Bradycardia / arrhythmia: Can occur with rapid IV injection — give over several minutes
• Circulatory shock: Paradoxical, typically from rapid administration or hypersensitivity
• Edema: Sodium/fluid retention from mineralocorticoid-like effects
• Hiccups: A recognized corticosteroid adverse effect, mechanism unclear
