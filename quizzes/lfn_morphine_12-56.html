⚠️ SINGLE INDICATION — Pain Management Only

LFN 12.56 lists one indication: Pain Management. Morphine's hemodynamic effects (venodilation, ↓ venous return) may benefit certain patients clinically, but there is no separate "Pulmonary Edema" or "ACS" dose listed in this protocol section. Dose and repeat interval are the same regardless of clinical context.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Acts on opiate receptors in the brain, providing both analgesia and sedation. Increases peripheral venous capacitance and decreases venous return.

🤰 Pregnancy

Morphine crosses the placenta. Opioids used during labor and delivery may temporarily affect the fetal heart rate. When opioids are needed to treat acute pain in pregnant patients, the lowest effective dose for only the expected duration of pain should be prescribed.

⏱️ ONSET — 30 Seconds (Faster Than You Think)

LFN 12.56 lists onset at 30 seconds IV — this is the time to first CNS effect. Full peak effect at 20 minutes. Duration 2–4 hours. Compare to fentanyl (1–2 min onset, 30 min duration) and hydromorphone (15 min onset, longer duration).

💉 Venodilation — The Hemodynamic Effect

Morphine's LFN MOA explicitly states it "increases peripheral venous capacitance and decreases venous return." This preload reduction can be therapeutically useful (volume-overloaded CHF, pulmonary edema) but can also cause or worsen hypotension — particularly in volume-depleted patients. This is why LFN specifically warns about volume depleted patients.

📊 Equianalgesic Dosing — PCG 2.06

Per PCG 2.06 Pearl #4: 5 mg morphine = 75 mcg fentanyl = 1 mg hydromorphone. These conversions are approximate and intended for clinical reference when transitioning between agents. Always titrate to effect rather than relying solely on conversion math.

📋 PCG 2.06 KEY MONITORING REQUIREMENTS
  • ECG, BP, HR, SpO₂ must be continuously monitored in all patients receiving pain medications.
  • ETCO₂ must be monitored in patients receiving multiple doses of opiates or receiving sedative agents.
  • Respiratory depression may occur after the 2nd or 3rd appropriately administered dose — even in medication-tolerant patients.
  • Use caution when combining narcotics and benzodiazepines — deep sedation and/or apnea can occur.
  • Pre-administration of nausea medications should be considered when giving opioid analgesics.
🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Hypersensitivity to morphine or any component of the formulation.

2. Fetal acidosis / non-reassuring fetal tracing. Morphine crosses the placenta and may worsen fetal compromise in the setting of fetal distress. This contraindication does not prohibit use in pregnant patients generally — only when fetal compromise is present.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (2)

1. Volume depleted patients — morphine's venodilatory effect reduces venous return, worsening hypotension in patients who are already volume deplete. Assess volume status and treat hypotension aggressively.

2. Elderly patients — increased sensitivity to opioid effects; reduced hepatic and renal clearance prolongs duration; lower starting doses and careful titration required.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (4)

Nausea/vomiting; bradycardia; hypotension; respiratory depression.

• Nausea/vomiting — opioid stimulation of the chemoreceptor trigger zone (area postrema); consider prophylactic antiemetic
• Bradycardia — vagotonic effect; monitor HR
• Hypotension — venodilation ↓ venous return; worse in volume-depleted patients
• Respiratory depression — dose-dependent; ETCO₂ monitoring mandatory with repeated doses
