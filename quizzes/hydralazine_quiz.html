🔬 Mechanism of Action

Decreases blood pressure by exhibiting a peripheral-vasodilating effect through direct relaxation of vascular smooth muscle. Hydralazine acts primarily on arterioles (resistance vessels), not veins. This selective arteriolar dilation reduces afterload (SVR) → lowers blood pressure. However, the venous system is spared → preload is maintained → baroreceptors detect the drop in afterload and trigger reflex tachycardia.

🤰 Pregnancy — THE Core Indication

Hydralazine crosses the placenta. IV hydralazine is recommended for use in the management of acute onset, severe hypertension (SBP ≥160 mm Hg or DBP ≥110 mm Hg) with preeclampsia or eclampsia in pregnant and postpartum patients. The pregnancy section IS the indication — hydralazine in LFN is exclusively an OB antihypertensive. Also applies to postpartum patients (eclampsia can occur up to 6 weeks after delivery).

📋 NO NUMBERED SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

Hydralazine has no SCs listed in LFN 12.36. The clinical guidance is embedded in the dosing protocol (titration instructions, infusion option) and the extensive warnings/adverse reactions. The mixture (20 mg/100 mL) is listed separately.

🚨 REFLEX TACHYCARDIA — The Key Clinical Concern

Hydralazine dilates arterioles → afterload drops → baroreceptors detect the pressure change → sympathetic nervous system activates → reflex tachycardia + increased cardiac contractility. This is why CAD is a contraindication: the reflex tachycardia increases myocardial oxygen demand while the vasodilation may reduce coronary perfusion pressure → angina or MI. Also why tachycardia is listed as an adverse reaction.

🤰 OB CONTEXT — When to Use Hydralazine vs Labetalol vs Magnesium

Severe preeclampsia/eclampsia treatment:
• Magnesium sulfate (12.49): Seizure PREVENTION/TREATMENT — 6 g loading over 30 min, then 2 g/hr infusion. Treats the eclamptic seizure risk.
• Hydralazine (12.36) or Labetalol (12.43) or Nifedipine (12.60): BP CONTROL — treats the hypertension (SBP ≥160 / DBP ≥110). These are complementary, not interchangeable with magnesium.
• Severe preeclampsia often needs BOTH: magnesium for seizure prevention AND hydralazine/labetalol for BP control.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (3)

1. Hypersensitivity.
2. Coronary artery disease (CAD). Reflex tachycardia from vasodilation increases myocardial O₂ demand while reducing coronary perfusion pressure → risk of angina or MI. Use labetalol instead for preeclamptic patients with CAD (labetalol's beta-blockade prevents reflex tachycardia).
3. Mitral valvular rheumatic heart disease. Vasodilation in the setting of mitral valve disease can cause acute hemodynamic decompensation.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (4)

1. Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (reflex tachycardia worsens dynamic LVOT obstruction).
2. Hypotension caution (overshoot can harm both mother and fetus — reduced uteroplacental perfusion).
3. Cerebrovascular disease (rapid BP changes can worsen cerebral perfusion in stroke-prone patients).
4. CrCl <50 (renal impairment — hydralazine clearance is reduced, risk of accumulation).

🚫 Adverse Reactions (12) — Extensive List

Cardiovascular: Hypotension (severe), tachycardia, angina, palpitations.
Hematologic: Neutropenia, blood dyscrasias.
Autoimmune: Lupus erythematosus (drug-induced lupus — rare but classic association).
Neurologic: Peripheral neuritis, headache.
Other: Hypersensitivity reaction, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.

Drug-induced lupus is unique to hydralazine among LFN drugs — more common with chronic use but worth knowing.
