📋 EGA Window Discrepancy in Protocol

The dosing table and SC #1 state 24 0/7 – 36 6/7 weeks. The pregnancy considerations section recommends a single course for patients between 24 0/7 – 33 6/7 weeks. The broader 36 6/7 window in the dosing table represents the LFN standing order range. The narrower 33 6/7 in pregnancy considerations reflects the strongest evidence base. Both are in the same formulary entry — use the dosing table/SC criteria for standing orders.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Exact mechanism of anti-inflammatory action unknown; inhibits multiple inflammatory cytokines; produces multiple glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid effects. The fetal lung maturation benefit comes from glucocorticoid stimulation of surfactant production in type II pneumocytes, structural maturation of lung tissue, and enhanced fluid clearance mechanisms.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Antenatal corticosteroid administration promotes fetal lung maturity and is associated with reduction of intraventricular hemorrhage, necrotizing enterocolitis, neonatal mortality, and respiratory distress syndrome. A single course is recommended for patients between 24 0/7 and 33 6/7 weeks\u2019 gestation at risk of delivering within 7 days. This recommendation includes those with ruptured membranes or multiple gestations.

  • Given via physician order for patients with an EGA of 24 0/7 weeks and 36 6/7 weeks AND there is a risk of preterm delivery within 7 days. This is not a standing field order \u2014 requires physician authorization.
  • Do not give if patient has already received a course of steroids. Multiple courses of antenatal steroids are associated with reduced fetal growth, smaller head circumference, and potential neurodevelopmental effects. One course only.
📋 COMPLETE COURSE = TWO DOSES

A complete course of betamethasone is two doses of 12 mg IM, given 24 hours apart. LFN protocol addresses the single-dose administration during transport. The second dose is typically given at the receiving facility 24 hours later. Maximum benefit occurs when delivery happens 24 hours to 7 days after the first dose. Even a single dose provides partial benefit if delivery occurs sooner.

\ud83d\udc76 RUPTURED MEMBRANES + MULTIPLES = Still Indicated

Per Pregnancy Considerations: "This recommendation includes those with ruptured membranes or multiple gestations." PPROM does NOT contraindicate betamethasone \u2014 in fact, PPROM patients are at HIGH risk for preterm delivery and benefit significantly. For multiples (twins, triplets), the standard single dose benefits all fetuses (steroids cross the placenta). No dose adjustment needed for multiple gestations.

\ud83d\udeab CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity. True allergy to betamethasone or any component of the formulation.

\u26a0\ufe0f Warnings / Precautions (3)

1. May cause hypercortisolism or suppression of hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Single dose in acute setting is unlikely to cause significant HPA suppression, but relevant for repeated dosing.
2. Use with caution in patients with heart failure and/or hypertension. Mineralocorticoid effects cause sodium/water retention, potentially worsening CHF and HTN.
3. Use with caution in patients with diabetes mellitus. Corticosteroids cause hyperglycemia through increased gluconeogenesis and insulin resistance.

\ud83d\udeab Adverse Reactions (10)

Hyperglycemia; bradycardia; cardiac arrhythmia; abnormal sensory symptoms; arachnoiditis; depression; emotional lability; euphoria; headache; increased intracranial pressure. Hyperglycemia is most clinically relevant for transport (monitor glucose in diabetics). Neuropsychiatric effects (depression, emotional lability, euphoria) are steroid class effects. Bradycardia and arrhythmia are rare but listed.

\ud83d\udcca Timing of Steroid Benefit

Maximum benefit: delivery 24 hours to 7 days after first dose. Even partial benefit if delivery occurs <2 hours after administration. Benefit diminishes after 7 days as steroid effect wanes. This is why the 7-day delivery risk criterion exists \u2014 giving steroids to a stable patient not expected to deliver soon wastes the one course allowed.
