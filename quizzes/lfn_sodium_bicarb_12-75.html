⚠️ Salicylate/Barbiturate OD: Adult dose is 2 mEq/kg (NOT 1 mEq/kg)

The hyperkalemia dose (1 mEq/kg, max 50 mEq) and the salicylate/barbiturate OD dose (2 mEq/kg) are different and a common source of confusion. For overdoses requiring urinary alkalinization, a higher initial dose is needed:

Hyperkalemia: 1 mEq/kg IV (max 50 mEq) — shifts K+ intracellularly
Salicylate/Barbiturate OD: 2 mEq/kg IV + infusion 250 mL/hr — requires a more aggressive alkalinization to trap ionized drug in urine for excretion

Both peds and adult salicylate/barb OD = "Same as Adult." The 150 mEq in 850 mL D5W mixture and the 250 mL/hr rate apply to the infusion portion (after the initial bolus).

🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"It immediately raises the pH of blood plasma by buffering excess hydrogen ions (acidosis). In a short time, the plasma alkali reserve is increased, and excess sodium and bicarbonate ions are excreted in urine, thus rendering the urine less acidic."

📝 MOA Clinical Application by Indication

Hyperkalemia: Alkalinizing the plasma drives a shift of potassium from the extracellular to intracellular compartment (K+ moves in to exchange for H+ moving out). This is a temporary redistribution — K+ total body burden is unchanged. Onset 2–10 min; duration 30–60 min.

Salicylate/Barbiturate OD: The LFN MOA specifically notes urinary effects: "rendering the urine less acidic." Alkaline urine traps ionized (charged) forms of weak acids (aspirin, barbiturates) in the tubular lumen — ionized drug cannot be reabsorbed → enhanced renal excretion. This is "ion trapping."

TCA overdose: TCAs are sodium channel blockers. Alkalinization (↑ serum pH) directly reverses sodium channel blockade — the high pH increases sodium availability at the channel and reduces TCA-channel binding affinity. The result is QRS narrowing and improved conduction. The LFN MOA does not mechanistically elaborate the TCA reversal — it describes the general buffering action.

Entrapment/Rhabdomyolysis: Alkaline urine prevents myoglobin (released from damaged muscle) from precipitating in renal tubules, protecting against myoglobin-induced acute tubular necrosis. Prevention requires administration AFTER NS bolus (to ensure adequate urine flow before alkalinizing).

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

"Medications used for the treatment of cardiac arrest in pregnancy are the same as in the nonpregnant woman. Doses and indications should follow current ACLS. Appropriate medications should not be withheld due to concerns of fetal teratogenicity."

The LFN 12.75 pregnancy text is framed around cardiac arrest only — consistent with the SC #1 restriction that bicarb is only used in arrest for hyperkalemia or TCA OD. The same ACLS-alignment framework appears in multiple LFN formulary entries; the key principle is that maternal survival requires appropriate treatment without dose modification or drug withholding due to pregnancy.

📋 Special Considerations (1)
1
"Routine administration during a cardiac arrest is NOT indicated unless suspected cause of arrest is hyperkalemia and/or TCA overdose."

LFN 12.75 has one SC. It does not say sodium bicarbonate is never given in cardiac arrest — it says routine administration is not indicated. The two specific exceptions:
• Hyperkalemia-induced arrest: Bicarb helps shift K+ in and stabilize the cardiac conduction system
• TCA overdose-induced arrest: Bicarb reverses sodium channel blockade that is driving the arrest

The LFN Warnings/Precautions elaborates: "Should not be given during cardiac arrest outside of hyperkalemia or known overdoses." The SC establishes the default; the warnings confirm the restriction.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Hypersensitivity

2. Pediatric patients as it may cause cerebral edema

The peds CI is notable: sodium bicarbonate is listed as contraindicated in pediatric patients as a general rule due to the risk of cerebral edema — but the formulary simultaneously provides detailed pediatric dosing for specific indications (hyperkalemia, TCA OD, salicylate OD, entrapment). The CI is a default caution; the specific indication doses establish the exceptions. This means: do not give bicarb routinely to peds patients, but it IS appropriate for the specific indications with the listed doses.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (3 elements)

1. "Can cause metabolic acidosis when given in large quantities."
Paradoxically, sodium bicarbonate can worsen acidosis when given in excess — large amounts of bicarb push the CO₂/HCO₃⁻ system toward excess bicarbonate, which then depletes H+ and can generate paradoxical intracellular acidosis via CO₂ diffusion.

2. "Should not be given during cardiac arrest outside of hyperkalemia or known overdoses."
Confirms SC #1 within the warnings text itself.

3. "Rarely indicated in DKA."
DKA acidosis resolves with insulin and fluids. Bicarb in DKA risks: paradoxical CNS acidosis, worsened hypokalemia, cerebral edema (especially in peds), and delayed recovery. It may be considered in extremis (pH <6.9 + cardiovascular instability), but it is explicitly "rarely indicated" — not "not indicated."

⚠️ Adverse Reactions (1)

"Severe tissue damage if extravagated."

Only one adverse reaction listed. LFN 12.75 spells it "extravagated" (not "extravasated") — the LFN formulary uses this spelling. Sodium bicarbonate is a highly alkaline solution; extravasation into tissue causes chemical burns and necrosis from the caustic alkaline pH.

One adverse reaction reflecting one primary non-systemic harm pathway. Systemic adverse effects (hypernatremia, metabolic alkalosis, hypokalemia) appear in the warnings as the metabolic acidosis/large quantities concern but are not separately listed as adverse reactions in 12.75.
