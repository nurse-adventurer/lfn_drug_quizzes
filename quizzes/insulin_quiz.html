🔑 CRITICAL: K+ ≥ 3.3 mEq/L Before DKA Insulin

Insulin drives K+ into cells. Starting insulin with low K+ → life-threatening hypokalemia → cardiac arrhythmias → arrest. Correct the potassium FIRST. This requirement applies to DKA (both adult and peds). The hyperkalemia and BB/CCB indications are treating elevated K+ or are potassium-independent.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Responsible for promoting the uptake of glucose by the cells. When administered, it is distributed throughout the body. It combines with insulin receptors present on the cell membranes. This promotes glucose entry into the cell. Insulin activates GLUT4 transporters, facilitating glucose movement from blood into cells. It also activates Na+/K+-ATPase, driving potassium into cells (this is why it treats hyperkalemia AND why K+ must be checked before DKA treatment).

💊 WHY 3 DIFFERENT DOSES? — Different Mechanisms for Different Indications

DKA (0.1 u/kg): Restores normal glucose metabolism, reverses ketogenesis. Goal: controlled glucose reduction + resolve acidosis. Low dose prevents rapid osmotic shifts.

Hyperkalemia (10 u): Activates Na+/K+-ATPase → drives K+ into cells. Goal: rapid K+ lowering. Fixed dose because the target is K+ shift, not glucose control.

BB/CCB OD (1 u/kg — 10× DKA dose): High-Dose Insulin Euglycemia Therapy (HIET). Provides glucose as myocardial energy substrate when calcium channels/beta receptors are blocked. The heart switches to glucose as its primary fuel. Goal: restore cardiac contractility/output.

🤰 Pregnancy

Due to pregnancy-induced physiologic changes, insulin requirements tend to increase as pregnancy progresses, requiring frequent monitoring and dosage adjustments. Following delivery, insulin requirements decrease rapidly. Insulin is the preferred treatment of type 1 and type 2 diabetes mellitus in pregnancy, as well as gestational diabetes mellitus when pharmacologic therapy is needed.

📊 SC #1 — BGL REDUCTION RATE: <100 mg/dL/hour

Blood glucose level should be reduced at a rate <100 mg/dL/hour. Rapid glucose reduction causes rapid osmotic shifts → water moves into brain cells → cerebral edema (especially dangerous in pediatric DKA). This is also why peds DKA has NO bolus (avoids rapid initial glucose drop). Frequent BGL checks (q 30-60 min) are essential to monitor the reduction rate.

📊 SC #2 — FREQUENT GLUCOSE MONITORING

Frequent blood glucose checks must be performed. This applies to ALL three indications: DKA (monitor reduction rate), hyperkalemia (watch for hypoglycemia from insulin + D10), BB/CCB OD (high-dose insulin causes significant glucose shifts requiring aggressive dextrose supplementation). In all cases, insulin lowers glucose — check frequently and have D10 ready.

💉 D10 CO-ADMINISTRATION RULES — When to Give Dextrose with Insulin

Hyperkalemia: Give with D10 if BGL <250 mg/dL. If BGL ≥250, D10 not required (sufficient glucose buffer).
BB/CCB OD: Give with D10 if BGL <250 mg/dL. High-dose insulin depletes glucose rapidly — expect significant D10 requirements.
DKA: Per dextrose 12.19 hyperkalemia protocol, D10 is co-administered. However, in DKA itself, the patient is already hyperglycemic — D10 would be contraindicated until glucose approaches 250.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Hypersensitivity.
2. Hypoglycemia. Never give insulin to a hypoglycemic patient — it will worsen the condition. Always check BGL before insulin administration.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (2)

1. Potassium level must be >3.3 mEq/L before administration (for DKA). Insulin drives K+ into cells — starting with low K+ → fatal hypokalemia → cardiac arrest.

2. Insulin may be adsorbed into the container or tubing when added to an IV infusion solution. This means the delivered dose may be less than intended. Prime tubing thoroughly and monitor response carefully.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (2)

Hypertension; tachycardia. Both are relatively mild compared to the main clinical risks of insulin therapy, which are: hypoglycemia (from the glucose-lowering effect) and hypokalemia (from the K+-shifting effect). These are predictable consequences of the mechanism, not idiosyncratic reactions.
