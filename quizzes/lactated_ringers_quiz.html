🔬 How LR Works — No Formal MOA Listed

LFN 12.44 does not list a formal mechanism of action (it's a crystalloid fluid, not a drug with a receptor target). LR works by expanding intravascular volume — the balanced electrolyte solution distributes throughout the extracellular fluid space. Approximately 25-30% of infused LR remains intravascular after 1 hour; the remainder distributes to the interstitial space. The lactate component is metabolized hepatically to bicarbonate, providing a mild buffering effect.

🤰 Pregnancy

May be used in pregnancy. One of the simplest pregnancy considerations in the formulary — LR closely mimics physiologic extracellular fluid composition.

💧 TITRATE TO DESIRED EFFECT — No Fixed Dose Protocol

Unlike most drugs in the formulary, LR dosing is "titrate to desired effect per patient presentation." The 250-500 mL (adult) and 10-20 mL/kg (peds) are typical bolus ranges, not fixed doses. Reassess after each bolus: vitals, lung sounds, mental status, urine output. The goal is adequate perfusion without fluid overload.

🧪 LACTATE → BICARBONATE: The Metabolic Transformation

Lactate is metabolized to bicarbonate by the liver. This is both a feature and a risk: in acidotic patients, the lactate provides buffering (helpful). In alkalotic patients, the lactate → bicarb conversion worsens the alkalosis (harmful). This is the single most clinically important metabolic fact about LR — it determines when to use LR vs NS.

💧 WHEN TO USE LR vs NS — Clinical Decision Points

Prefer LR when: General resuscitation, trauma, sepsis (balanced electrolytes, less hyperchloremic acidosis than NS), burn patients, surgical patients.

Prefer NS when: Hyperkalemia (NS has no K+), metabolic alkalosis (NS won't add bicarbonate), neonates ≤28 days (LR contraindicated), patients requiring blood product compatibility (some institutions), mixing with certain medications.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Hypersensitivity to sodium lactate or any component.
2. Neonates (≤28 days). Immature hepatic function → cannot adequately metabolize lactate. Immature renal function → cannot handle electrolyte load. Use NS instead for neonatal fluid resuscitation.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (4 Key Populations)

1. Alkalosis: "Administer with extreme caution, if at all." Lactate → bicarbonate worsens metabolic alkalosis.

2. Heart failure: Risk of fluid overload. Compromised cardiac function cannot handle rapid volume expansion. Use smaller boluses (250 mL) and reassess frequently.

3. Hyperkalemia: "Use with extreme caution, if at all." LR contains ~4 mEq/L potassium. Also caution with conditions predisposing to hyperK: severe kidney impairment, adrenocortical insufficiency, acute dehydration, extensive tissue injury or burns.

4. Renal impairment: Risk of electrolyte retention (K+ and Na+). Impaired excretion leads to accumulation.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (2)

Fluid overload; potassium and/or sodium retention in patients with renal impairment. Both are dose-dependent — smaller, more frequent boluses with reassessment between reduce risk.
