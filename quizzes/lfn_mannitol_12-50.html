🔬 Mechanism of Action — Two-Phase Process

Phase 1 — Osmotic Draw: Through its hypertonic effects, draws water into the intravascular space (reduces brain volume and ICP).

Phase 2 — Osmotic Diuresis: Raises osmotic pressure of glomerular filtrate, thereby inhibiting tubular reabsorption of water and solutes — excreting the fluid load via urine.

Net result: Fluid is pulled OUT of brain tissue → into blood → excreted in urine → brain volume decreases → ICP falls.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Mannitol crosses the placenta and may decrease amniotic fluid volume. This is clinically relevant in OB patients requiring mannitol — fetal monitoring and obstetric consultation should be prioritized. Both indications (DKA cerebral edema, herniation) can occur in pregnancy.

📋 SC #1 — FILTER REQUIRED FOR ALL ADMINISTRATION

Concentrations greater than 15% have a greater tendency to crystallize; an in-line IV filter must be used for administration.

Standard mannitol preparations (20% or 25%) exceed this threshold and will crystallize, especially when cold. A filter prevents crystal emboli. This requirement applies to EVERY administration — not only when crystals are visible.

📋 SC #2 — DO NOT ADMINISTER WITH BLOOD PRODUCTS

Mannitol must NOT be co-administered with blood products. Mannitol is hyperosmolar and can cause red blood cell crenation (shrinkage) and hemolysis when mixed with blood. Use a separate line or flush thoroughly between products.

⚠️ SC #3 — REBOUND ICP ELEVATION at ~12 HOURS

Rebound increases in ICP can occur approximately 12 hours post-administration.

If mannitol crosses a damaged blood-brain barrier, it can accumulate in brain tissue and create a REVERSE osmotic gradient — drawing water BACK into the brain. This is why mannitol is ideally a bridge to definitive care, and why the ICP-lowering benefit is time-limited. Plan for receiving facility to manage this window.

⚠️ RAPID ADMINISTRATION WARNING — Transient Volume Overload

Rapid administration can cause a transitory increase in intravascular volume and can result in CHF.

The protocol specifies 15 minutes for a reason. Before the diuresis phase kicks in, mannitol draws a large fluid volume into the vasculature. In patients with compromised cardiac function, this initial "pre-diuresis" expansion can precipitate acute pulmonary edema — which is why CHF and marked pulmonary edema are CIs.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (6)

Marked pulmonary edema or CHF; organic CNS disease; intracranial bleeding; shock; severe dehydration; history of allergy.

• Pulmonary edema/CHF: Transient volume expansion from Phase 1 → worsens cardiac overload
• Organic CNS disease: BBB disruption allows mannitol into brain → reverse gradient → rebound ICP
• Intracranial bleeding: Reducing brain volume may remove tamponade effect on active bleed
• Shock: Diuresis worsens hypovolemia and reduces CPP
• Severe dehydration: Cannot tolerate additional osmotic fluid loss
• History of allergy: Mannitol can cause allergic/anaphylactic reactions

⚠️ Warning / Precaution

Rapid administration can cause a transitory increase in intravascular volume, which can result in CHF.

Always administer over the full 15 minutes. Do not push rapidly even in herniation emergencies — the protocol rate IS the emergency rate.

🚫 Adverse Reactions

CHF; hypotension; hypertension; thrombophlebitis; fluid and electrolyte imbalance (especially hyponatremia); dehydration; acidosis; with extravasation: local edema, skin necrosis; chills; fever; allergic reactions.

These span three mechanisms:
• Volume shifts: CHF (pre-diuresis), hypotension (post-diuresis), dehydration
• Electrolytes: Hyponatremia (dilutional from osmotic shift), acidosis
• Local/vascular: Thrombophlebitis, extravasation necrosis (mannitol is hypertonic — use patent IV)
