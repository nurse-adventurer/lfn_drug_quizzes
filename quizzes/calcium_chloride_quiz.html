🔬 Mechanism of Action

Causes significant increase in myocardial contractility and in ventricular automaticity. In hyperkalemia, calcium raises the threshold potential of cardiac myocytes, restoring the normal gradient between resting and threshold potentials — this "stabilizes" the membrane against arrhythmogenic effects of elevated K⁺. In CCB overdose, high-dose calcium overcomes competitive blockade through mass action (overwhelming concentration gradient). In hypocalcemia/MTP, it directly replaces the deficient electrolyte.

🤰 Pregnancy

Medications used as antidotes should be administered to pregnant women if there is a clear indication and should not be withheld because of fears of teratogenicity. Follow current ACLS guidelines.

  • 1 gram of calcium chloride = 270 mg of elemental calcium. This is the key conversion factor. Know this number for dosing equivalence calculations.
  • Calcium chloride delivers 3× more elemental calcium than calcium gluconate. 1 g CaCl₂ = ~270 mg elemental Ca. 1 g CaGluconate = ~90 mg elemental Ca. Not interchangeable at same doses.
  • Administer via a large patent IV or central line due to risk of tissue necrosis secondary to extravasation. CaCl₂ is highly hypertonic (~2000 mOsm/L) and caustic to tissues. Avoid hand veins and small peripheral IVs.
  • The need for calcium is optimally based on serum ionized calcium levels. Ionized Ca²⁺ is the physiologically active form. Total calcium can be misleading (albumin binding). Target iCa >1.0 mmol/L.
🚨 EXTRAVASATION — Most Critical Administration Risk

CaCl₂ extravasation can cause full-thickness tissue necrosis requiring skin grafting. The hypertonic solution draws fluid from tissues while Ca²⁺ causes protein denaturation and cell death. The dorsum of the hand is particularly vulnerable (minimal subcutaneous tissue). In emergency with poor IV access: use what you have, ensure blood return, administer slowly, and watch the site meticulously. Stop immediately if ANY infiltration suspected.

📋 MTP Context — Per PCG 8.02 Blood Product Administration

Per PCG 8.02: Calcium gluconate 1–2 grams IV over 5 minutes after 1–2 units given (include PTA). The blood product PCG specifically references calcium gluconate, while the formulary also lists calcium chloride for MTP. Either may be used, but be aware of the 3× potency difference. Citrate in blood products chelates ionized calcium → hypocalcemia → ↓ contractility + coagulopathy.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Ventricular fibrillation — Calcium worsens intracellular Ca²⁺ overload during VF, making defibrillation harder. Exception: VF caused by hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, or CCB toxicity.
2. Hypercalcemia — Adding calcium when levels are already high → fatal cardiac arrest.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Patients on digitalis — calcium potentiates digoxin toxicity (both increase intracellular Ca²⁺). Use with extreme caution: slower rate, smaller doses, continuous ECG monitoring.
Renal or cardiac insufficiency — impaired calcium clearance and increased sensitivity.
Immobilized patients — already at risk for hypercalcemia from bone resorption.
High concentrations reaching the heart can cause fatal cardiac arrest — give SLOWLY.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (6)

Tingling sensations; hypotension; bradycardia and other dysrhythmias; syncope; cardiac arrest; local reactions. Bradycardia can occur from calcium's effect on cardiac conduction. Rapid administration → severe hypotension or cardiac arrest. "Local reactions" = the tissue necrosis risk from extravasation.
