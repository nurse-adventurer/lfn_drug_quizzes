🔬 Mechanism of Action

Stabilizes neuronal membranes and decreases seizure activity by decreasing the influx of sodium ions across cell membranes in the motor cortex during generation of nerve impulses. Converts to phenytoin after administration. Fosphenytoin is a water-soluble phosphate ester PRODRUG of phenytoin. After IV administration, phosphatases cleave the phosphate group, releasing active phenytoin. This prodrug approach allows faster infusion (150 mg/min) with less injection-site reactions than direct phenytoin IV.

🧠 WHERE FOSPHENYTOIN FITS — Status Epilepticus Treatment Ladder

Step 1: Benzodiazepines (midazolam, diazepam, lorazepam) — first-line for seizure termination
Step 2: If benzos fail → Fosphenytoin 15-20 mg PE/kg OR levetiracetam — second-line for sustained seizure control
Step 3: If Step 2 fails → Ketamine (12.42: 1 mg/kg q 5 min, max 5 mg/kg for refractory seizures)
Fosphenytoin provides sustained anticonvulsant effect (Na+ channel blockade) that benzos alone cannot maintain.

🤰 Pregnancy

Fosphenytoin is the prodrug of phenytoin. An increased risk of congenital malformations and adverse outcomes may occur following in utero phenytoin exposure. However, uncontrolled status epilepticus also carries extreme risk to mother and fetus. In SE, the benefit of seizure termination outweighs the teratogenic risk.

📊 SC #1 — PHENYTOIN EQUIVALENTS (PE) — The Key Dosing Concept

Dose is expressed in mg phenytoin equivalents (PE). 1 mg PE = 1 mg phenytoin sodium.

Why PE units exist: fosphenytoin is a prodrug — 1.5 mg of fosphenytoin converts to 1 mg of phenytoin. Rather than making clinicians do this conversion, dosing is expressed in PE units so that the dose number directly corresponds to the phenytoin dose you're delivering. If you need a 20 mg/kg phenytoin load, you order 20 mg PE/kg of fosphenytoin. Simple.

⚠️ INFUSION RATE — The Critical Safety Limit

Max rate: 150 mg PE/min. Exceeding this rate causes hypotension from phenytoin's cardiovascular effects (sodium channel blockade in the heart → vasodilation + myocardial depression). Phenytoin-related hypotension can cause cardiac arrest in severe cases. Despite the urgency of status epilepticus, NEVER exceed 150 mg PE/min.

Infusion time examples:
• 1000 mg PE ÷ 150 mg/min = ~7 minutes
• 1500 mg PE ÷ 150 mg/min = ~10 minutes
• 2000 mg PE ÷ 150 mg/min = ~13 minutes

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (3)

1. Second- or third-degree AV block — phenytoin's sodium channel blockade further depresses AV conduction → complete heart block or asystole.
2. Sinus bradycardia — further conduction depression can cause symptomatic bradycardia or arrest.
3. Seizures due to hypoglycemia — the seizure is caused by inadequate brain glucose, not abnormal electrical activity. Treat with dextrose (12.19), not anticonvulsants. Fosphenytoin won't help AND may increase glucose further.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Use with caution in patients with hypotension and myocardial insufficiency. Phenytoin causes vasodilation and myocardial depression — in already compromised patients, this can be catastrophic. May increase serum glucose (phenytoin inhibits insulin release from pancreatic beta cells). May cause hepatic impairment.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (3)

Dizziness; headache; hypotension (if given at rate >150 mg/min PE). The hypotension is rate-dependent and the most clinically significant adverse reaction. Monitor BP during infusion and slow or stop if hypotension develops. The dizziness and headache relate to CNS effects of phenytoin.
