💊 THE UNIQUE DOSING RULE — "Whichever Is Higher"

20 mg OR amount equal to patient's daily dose, whichever is higher. This is one of the most unique dosing rules in the LFN formulary. It accounts for diuretic tolerance in chronic users. A patient taking 80 mg/day of furosemide at home has tolerance — 20 mg would be ineffective. Give them their home dose (80 mg). A patient on no diuretics gets the standard 20 mg.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Blocks the absorption of sodium and chloride in the kidney tubules and the Loop of Henle, causing a profound increase in urine output. The Loop of Henle reabsorbs ~25% of filtered sodium — blocking this site produces the most potent diuresis of any diuretic class. Additionally, furosemide causes venodilation (preload reduction) that begins almost immediately — even before diuresis starts. This early venodilation is why patients often feel improvement within minutes of IV furosemide.

💧 TWO MECHANISMS OF PRELOAD REDUCTION

Immediate (seconds-minutes): Venodilation — increases venous capacitance, reducing preload. Patient feels better BEFORE they start urinating. This is why the SBP >100 requirement exists — even before diuresis, venodilation lowers BP.

Delayed (5-30 min): Diuresis — removes fluid volume through the kidneys. Na+/Cl-/H₂O excretion. Also excretes K+ in the distal tubule (causing hypokalemia adverse reaction).

🤰 Pregnancy

Furosemide crosses the placenta. May be used for symptom management in pregnant patients with heart failure complicated by pulmonary congestion; closely monitor volume status and adjust dose to minimize risk of placental hypoperfusion. Aggressive diuresis can reduce uteroplacental blood flow.

🔊 SC #1 — OTOTOXICITY: Tinnitus and Hearing Loss

High doses or rapid administration may cause tinnitus and or hearing loss. Furosemide affects the stria vascularis in the inner ear, disrupting ion transport essential for hearing. This is dose-dependent and rate-dependent — give slowly to reduce risk. Usually reversible but can be permanent, especially with concurrent ototoxic drugs (aminoglycosides, vancomycin, cisplatin). If tinnitus develops during administration, SLOW the push rate.

📋 CHF TREATMENT CONTEXT — Where Furosemide Fits

Acute CHF/pulmonary edema treatment approach:
1. Position: Sit upright (reduces preload)
2. Oxygen/CPAP/BiPAP: Per PCG 8.23 (SBP >100 for NIV)
3. Nitroglycerin: 0.4 mg SL (SBP >100) — vasodilation reduces preload
4. Furosemide: 20 mg or home dose (SBP >100) — diuresis + venodilation
5. Milrinone: 12.55 — inotrope/vasodilator for severe CHF
Note: the SBP >100 threshold appears in BOTH NTG and furosemide protocols — hypotensive pulmonary edema needs inotropes, not vasodilators/diuretics.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (1)

Anuria. Single, simple CI. If the kidneys are not producing urine, furosemide cannot reach its site of action (Loop of Henle) and will accumulate systemically → increased ototoxicity risk with no therapeutic benefit. Note: oliguria (low output) is NOT the same as anuria (no output) — furosemide may still work in oliguria.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

In pediatric patients with pulmonary edema, use caution in the absence of an inotrope. Children with CHF are often more preload-dependent than adults — aggressive diuresis without inotropic support can cause cardiovascular collapse. No peds standing order reflects this caution. The peds warning combined with no standing order means: if you give furosemide to a child, it requires physician order AND concurrent inotropic support.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (3)

Dehydration; dizziness; hypokalemia. All are predictable consequences of profound diuresis: fluid loss → dehydration + orthostatic dizziness; Na+/K+ exchange in the distal tubule → K+ wasting → hypokalemia. Monitor electrolytes and volume status. The hypokalemia is clinically significant because it can trigger cardiac arrhythmias, especially in patients on digoxin.

⚡ FUROSEMIDE + DROPERIDOL — A Dangerous Drug Interaction

Furosemide causes hypokalemia. Droperidol (12.25) prolongs QT. Hypokalemia + QT prolongation = dramatically increased risk of Torsades de Pointes. Per droperidol warnings: "Use extreme caution with electrolyte disturbances (hypokalemia), including concomitant drugs which may alter electrolytes (diuretics)." If a patient received furosemide and later needs droperidol for agitation, the hypokalemia risk must be considered.
