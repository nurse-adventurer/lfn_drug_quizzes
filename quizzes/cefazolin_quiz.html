📋 PCG 7.01 General Trauma Care — Cefazolin Context

Per PCG 7.01 CCT section: "For open fractures, consider cefazolin 2 grams IV over 3–5 minutes." Peds: 30 mg/kg IV, max 2 grams. "All trauma and other stabilization care must be completed prior to administration." Also: "May be given for crush injuries or severe tissue trauma with physician order."

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to one or more of the penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs) which in turn inhibits the final transpeptidation step of peptidoglycan synthesis in bacterial cell walls, thus inhibiting cell wall biosynthesis. First-generation cephalosporins provide excellent gram-positive coverage (MSSA, coag-negative staph, streptococci) and some gram-negative coverage (E. coli, Klebsiella). Does NOT cover MRSA, Pseudomonas, Enterococcus, or anaerobes.

🤰 Pregnancy

Based on available data, cephalosporin antibiotics are generally considered compatible for use during pregnancy. One of the safer antibiotic classes in pregnancy — do not withhold for indicated open fracture prophylaxis.

  • Reconstitute 1 gram vial with 2.5 mL of normal saline or sterile water. For 2 g adult dose: reconstitute two vials. Creates an injectable solution for slow IVP.
  • May be given via slow IV push over 3–5 minutes OR mixed in 100 mL of saline and infused. Two administration options: direct IVP (practical for field/scene) or piggyback infusion (practical during transport with pump).
  • May also be given for crush injuries or other severe tissue trauma with physician's order. This extends the indication beyond open fractures — but requires physician order (not a standing order for crush/tissue trauma).

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity to penicillin, amoxicillin, and any other cephalosporin. Cross-reactivity between penicillins and cephalosporins is ~1–10% (higher with true IgE-mediated reactions). If the patient has documented anaphylaxis, urticaria, or angioedema to ANY penicillin or cephalosporin — do NOT give cefazolin.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Hypersensitivity reactions, including anaphylaxis, may occur. If an allergic reaction occurs, discontinue treatment and institute appropriate supportive measures. Use with caution in patients with a history of penicillin allergy. The "use with caution" language acknowledges that not all penicillin allergy histories carry the same risk — mild, distant reactions are lower risk than recent severe reactions.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (6)

Skin rash; Stevens-Johnson syndrome; hypotension; syncope; abdominal cramps; nausea/vomiting. Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) is the most severe — a life-threatening mucocutaneous reaction. SJS is rare but listed. Skin rash is the most common adverse reaction. Hypotension can occur with rapid IV push (give over 3–5 min, not bolus).

📋 Allergy Spectrum — Not All PCN Allergies Are Equal

PCN allergy history ranges from "I got nauseous" (probably not true allergy) to "my throat closed and I needed epinephrine" (true IgE-mediated anaphylaxis). LFN lists penicillin allergy as a contraindication — but the warning section adds "use with caution in patients with a history of penicillin allergy." In clinical practice, contact medical control for borderline cases. Document allergy details and your risk-benefit reasoning.
