🔬 Mechanism of Action

Produces a rapid induction of anesthesia with minimal cardiovascular and respiratory effects. It is rapidly distributed following IV injection and rapidly metabolized and excreted. Etomidate enhances GABA-A receptor activity (similar to barbiturates and propofol), but with a uniquely favorable hemodynamic profile — minimal effect on heart rate, blood pressure, or cardiac output at induction doses.

⏱️ WHY 30-60 SEC / 3-5 MIN MATTERS

The ultra-fast onset (30-60 sec) makes etomidate ideal for RSI — unconsciousness occurs within a minute. But the ultra-short duration (3-5 min) means the patient WILL wake up if you don't have post-intubation sedation ready. This is the rationale for SC #2: "Prepare post-intubation sedation/analgesic medications prior to induction." A patient waking up with an ETT in place and no sedation is a clinical emergency.

🤰 Pregnancy

Etomidate crosses the placenta. However, it may be used in emergent situations when other agents are contraindicated. In OB emergencies requiring RSI, ketamine is generally preferred, but etomidate remains an option.

  • Etomidate has no analgesic effects. This means post-intubation care must include BOTH a sedative AND an analgesic. A patient who received etomidate for RSI and is now intubated needs pain control (fentanyl, ketamine) in addition to sedation (propofol, midazolam, dexmedetomidine). Etomidate alone does NOT treat pain.
  • Prepare post-intubation sedation/analgesic medications prior to induction due to short duration. With only 3-5 minutes of effect, etomidate wears off quickly. If post-intubation medications aren't drawn up and ready BEFORE you push etomidate, the patient will awaken with an ETT in place before you can get sedation started. Have propofol (or ketamine/midazolam) + fentanyl drawn up, dosed, and labeled before RSI begins.
🚨 SC #2 — THE 3-5 MINUTE CLOCK

The moment you push etomidate, a 3-5 minute clock starts. By the time you intubate (1-2 min), confirm placement (30 sec), and secure the tube (1 min), you have at most 1-2 minutes before the patient begins to lighten. Post-intubation sedation MUST be ready to push within 30-60 seconds of tube confirmation. Per PCG 8.27: "You must have post intubation sedation and analgesia medications drawn up and ready to administer after the ETT is secured due to the short duration of etomidate."

📋 PCG 8.27 RSI PREPARATION — Post-Intubation Plan Required

PCG 8.27 Step 1 (Preparation) specifically lists: "Post intubation sedation and analgesia plan in place" as a required pre-RSI step. This isn't optional — it's part of the RSI checklist. For etomidate RSI, have propofol infusion ready (25 mcg/kg/min with 1 mg/kg bolus) or ketamine (1 mg/kg bolus + 0.5 mg/kg q 15 min) + fentanyl (1-2 mcg/kg).

✅ CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity. Simple, single CI. Unlike many drugs, etomidate has very few absolute contraindications — its hemodynamic stability makes it usable in most patients (including hypotensive patients, with caution).

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (3)

1. Caution in patients with marked hypotension. Despite hemodynamic stability, etomidate CAN still cause some hypotension — in patients already hypotensive, even small decreases matter.

2. Caution with severe asthma. Etomidate doesn't directly cause bronchospasm, but it doesn't protect against it either (unlike ketamine which is bronchodilatory).

3. Causes adrenal suppression, particularly those with sepsis. Etomidate inhibits 11-beta-hydroxylase → blocks cortisol synthesis. Even a single dose suppresses adrenal function for 6-24 hours. In sepsis, cortisol is critical for maintaining vascular tone and hemodynamic stability. Consider ketamine instead for septic patients.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (6)

Apnea; hypotension; dysrhythmias; nausea/vomiting; myoclonic skeletal muscle movements; trismus. Myoclonus (involuntary jerking) is NOT seizure activity — it's a common, expected adverse effect from subcortical disinhibition. Trismus (jaw tightness) can complicate intubation — the paralytic overcomes this. Apnea is expected (that's why we're intubating).
