🔬 Mechanism of Action

Exerts direct effect on blood coagulation (clotting) by enhancing the inhibitory actions of antithrombin III (Heparin cofactor) on several factors essential to normal blood clotting, thereby blocking the conversion of prothrombin to thrombin and fibrinogen to fibrin. Heparin PREVENTS new clot formation and clot EXTENSION. It does NOT dissolve existing clots — that requires thrombolytics (tPA/alteplase 12.06, tenecteplase 12.79).

🤰 Pregnancy

Heparin does not cross the placenta — making it one of the safer anticoagulants in pregnancy. Some products contain benzyl alcohol preservative; use of a preservative-free formulation is recommended in pregnant patients.

🔄 SC #1 — PROTAMINE SULFATE REVERSAL

May be reversed with protamine sulfate. Protamine is a strongly basic protein that binds to acidic heparin, forming a stable inactive complex and neutralizing anticoagulation. Important for bleeding complications — know that the reversal agent exists and what it is. Protamine can itself cause hypotension and anaphylaxis, so give slowly.

🏥 DESTINATION MATTERS — Know Before You Dose

LFN has two different heparin protocols based on where the STEMI patient is going:

TSE center (standard): 60 u/kg bolus (max 4,000) + 12 u/kg/hr infusion (max 1,000/hr)
St. Charles Bend (specific): 70 u/kg bolus (max 10,000) — no infusion

The differences are significant: St. Charles uses a higher per-kg dose (70 vs 60), higher max (10,000 vs 4,000), and bolus-only (no infusion). These reflect the receiving facility's PCI protocol preferences. Confirm destination before drawing up heparin.

💉 PUMP CALCULATION — TSE Infusion

Mixture: 5,000 units / 100 mL = 50 units/mL.

Infusion rate conversion:
• 12 u/kg/hr for 80 kg = 960 u/hr
• 960 u/hr ÷ 50 u/mL = 19.2 mL/hr on the pump
• Max 1,000 u/hr ÷ 50 u/mL = 20 mL/hr maximum pump rate

Per PCG 2.01 #18a: "During handoffs at sending and receiving facilities, coordinate changing of infusions between pumps to minimize interruptions of any critical infusions."

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (3)

1. Hypersensitivity to heparin.
2. Active bleeding — anticoagulation worsens ongoing hemorrhage. Heparin prevents hemostasis → uncontrolled bleeding.
3. Bleeding tendencies (hemophilia, purpura, thrombocytopenia) — patients with coagulation disorders are at extreme risk with anticoagulation.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (4)

1. Aspirin and other NSAIDs increase the risk of bleeding — dual anticoagulant + antiplatelet therapy (heparin + aspirin) is standard for STEMI but significantly increases bleeding risk. Monitor closely.
2. Nitroglycerin IV may decrease anticoagulant activity — this interaction can reduce heparin's effectiveness. If running a NTG drip with heparin, anticoagulation may be subtherapeutic.
3. Protamine antagonizes effects of heparin — stated as a warning (not just SC #1) because accidental protamine administration negates anticoagulation.
4. History of intracranial hemorrhage — prior ICH indicates vulnerable cerebral vasculature; anticoagulation increases risk of recurrent hemorrhage.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (6)

Numbness and tingling of hands and feet; headache; spontaneous bleeding; cyanosis and pains in arms or legs (vasospasm); hypertension; chest pain. Spontaneous bleeding is the most clinically significant adverse reaction. The vasospasm symptoms (cyanosis, pain in extremities) are less common but important — can mimic arterial occlusion.
