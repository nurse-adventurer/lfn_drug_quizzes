🔬 Mechanism of Action

Inhibitor of platelet function. Aspirin irreversibly inhibits cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1), blocking thromboxane A₂ (TXA₂) synthesis. TXA₂ is a potent platelet aggregator and vasoconstrictor. By blocking TXA₂, aspirin prevents platelet clumping at the site of coronary plaque rupture, limiting further thrombus growth.

🤰 Pregnancy

Except when used in lower doses for pregnancy-related conditions, maternal use of aspirin should be avoided beginning 20 weeks gestation. Before 20 weeks: low-dose aspirin may be used (e.g., for preeclampsia prevention). At/after 20 weeks: standard-dose aspirin is contraindicated due to risks of premature closure of the ductus arteriosus and oligohydramnios.

📋 No Special Considerations Listed in LFN 12.08

Aspirin is one of the simplest drugs in the formulary — single dose (324 mg), single route (PO), two indications, adult only. No special considerations, no recommended mixture, no trade name listed in the formulary entry. However, several important clinical considerations apply from general practice:

💊 ADMINISTRATION — Chew vs Swallow

Chewing aspirin tablets provides faster absorption than swallowing whole. Chewing breaks the enteric coating (if present), exposes the drug to buccal/sublingual mucosa, and creates smaller particles for faster gastric absorption. In STEMI/ACS, where every minute of platelet inhibition matters, instruct the patient to CHEW the tablets. Time to platelet inhibition: chewed ~5 min vs swallowed ~12 min.

🔢 324 mg vs 325 mg — Why the Specific Number?

324 mg = 4 × 81 mg (baby aspirin) tablets. LFN specifies 324 mg because the standard low-dose aspirin tablet is 81 mg. Four of these = 324 mg, which is the practical dose available on the aircraft. Standard "adult" aspirin tablets are 325 mg, but most EMS and flight programs carry 81 mg chewable tablets for flexibility. The 1 mg difference is clinically insignificant.

❓ PRIOR ASPIRIN — Ask Before Giving

Always ask: "Have you taken aspirin today?" Many cardiac patients take daily aspirin (81 mg). If they took their daily dose, they still need the additional loading dose for acute STEMI/ACS. If EMS already gave 324 mg, document and communicate — don't re-dose. Also screen for aspirin/NSAID allergy and active GI bleeding before administration.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity to salicylates. This includes aspirin allergy AND aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD/Samter's triad: aspirin sensitivity + asthma + nasal polyps). True aspirin allergy can cause anaphylaxis; AERD causes severe bronchospasm.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (3)

1. Patients with allergies to other NSAIDs — cross-reactivity possible between NSAIDs and aspirin, especially in AERD.
2. Bleeding disorders — aspirin inhibits platelet function for up to 10 days, worsening any existing coagulopathy.
3. Severe liver disease — hepatic impairment affects aspirin metabolism and increases bleeding risk. Also associated with 2–4 fold increase in upper GI events (ulcers, bleeding, perforation).

🚫 Adverse Reactions (10)

Cardiac arrhythmia; hypotension; tachycardia; abdominal pain; dyspepsia; gastrointestinal perforation; gastrointestinal ulcer; heartburn; nausea; vomiting. The GI effects dominate — aspirin inhibits protective prostaglandins in the gastric mucosa, increasing ulcer and bleeding risk. Cardiac effects are less common but listed.

🤰 Pregnancy — The 20-Week Cutoff

Avoid beginning 20 weeks gestation (except low-dose for pregnancy-related conditions like preeclampsia prevention). After 20 weeks: risk of premature closure of fetal ductus arteriosus, oligohydramnios, and neonatal complications. Before 20 weeks: low-dose (81 mg) may be used for preeclampsia prevention — but this is NOT the 324 mg STEMI dose.
