💡 Equianalgesic Doses

75 mcg fentanyl = 1 mg hydromorphone = 5 mg morphine. These equivalences are approximate and useful for opioid rotation or dose comparison across the LFN formulary.

📊 Onset / Peak / Duration

Onset: 1–2 min  |  Peak: 3–5 min  |  Duration: 30 min
Rapid onset makes fentanyl ideal for acute pain in the transport environment. Short duration means frequent reassessment and redosing may be needed. Infusion addresses the short half-life for longer transports.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Binds with stereospecific receptors at many sites within the CNS. Increases pain threshold, alters pain reception, and inhibits ascending pain pathways. Fentanyl is a potent synthetic opioid agonist — approximately 100× more potent than morphine on a weight-for-weight basis.

⚠️ Adverse Reactions

May cause CNS depression, hypotension, and respiratory depression. These are the three critical adverse effects to monitor for — the triad of opioid toxicity.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Fentanyl crosses the placenta. IM and IV injection are commonly used to treat maternal pain during labor and immediately postpartum; the intranasal route has also been studied.

  • Consider reducing the dose in elderly patients and those with cardiovascular instability. Fentanyl's hemodynamic effects are amplified in these populations — start low and titrate carefully.
  • ETCO₂ must be monitored in patients receiving multiple doses of opiates or receiving sedative agents. This is a mandatory monitoring requirement per LFN protocol — not optional.
  • ECG, blood pressure, heart rate, SpO₂ must be continuously monitored in all patients receiving pain medications per PCG 2.06.
  • Consider reducing dose of analgesia in hypotensive and/or catecholamine-depleted states. Hypotension should be aggressively treated per PCG 2.06.
  • Pre-administration of nausea medications should be considered when giving opioid analgesics.
  • Respiratory depression may occur after the 2nd or 3rd appropriately administered dose even in medication-tolerant patients per PCG 2.06 Pearl #6.
  • Provide bolus dose prior to infusion AND with each titration. Do not start or uptitrate the infusion without a concurrent bolus.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity.

⚠️ Caution — Hypovolemia

Use with caution in patients with hypovolemia. Fentanyl can worsen hypotension in volume-depleted patients. Consider dose reduction and aggressive fluid resuscitation.

⚠️ CNS/Respiratory Depression Risk

Alcohol may potentiate CNS/respiratory depression effects. Use caution when combining narcotics and benzodiazepines — deep sedation and/or apnea can occur.

🫁 Adverse Reactions — Opioid Toxicity Triad

CNS depression, hypotension, and respiratory depression. These three effects represent the classic opioid toxicity triad. Monitor continuously and have naloxone available.

📋 Mandatory Monitoring

ETCO₂ must be monitored in patients receiving multiple doses of opiates or receiving sedative agents. Additionally, ECG, BP, HR, and SpO₂ must be continuously monitored in all patients receiving pain medications.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Fentanyl crosses the placenta. Commonly used for maternal pain during labor and postpartum via IM/IV; intranasal route also studied.

📋 RSI Pre-Treatment — Pediatric Only

Fentanyl 2 mcg/kg IV is indicated as RSI pre-treatment for pediatric patients when using midazolam for RSI induction. Hold if hypotensive. There is no adult standing order for fentanyl RSI pre-treatment.
