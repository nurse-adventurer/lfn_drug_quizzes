💡 When to Add Vasopressin

Vasopressin is a second-line agent — indicated only when shock/hypotension is refractory to norepinephrine.
Per Norepinephrine 12.63 Special Consideration #2: consider adding a second agent when norepinephrine rate > 30 mcg/min. After vasopressin is added, norepi may exceed the listed max of 45 mcg/min.

📊 Onset / Peak / Duration

Onset: 1–2 min  |  Peak: Not specified  |  Duration: 10–20 min
Rapid onset (1–2 min) allows quick assessment of hemodynamic response. Short duration (10–20 min) means continuous infusion is required — effects dissipate rapidly if infusion stops.

🔬 Mechanism of Action

Vasopressin produces concentrated urine by increasing tubular reabsorption of water (ADH activity), thus preserving up to 90% water. It may increase sodium and decrease potassium reabsorption but plays no causative role in edema formation.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Vasopressin may produce tonic uterine contractions. Due to pregnancy-induced physiologic changes, vasopressin clearance may be increased in the second and third trimesters.

  • Second-line vasopressor only: Vasopressin is indicated for shock/hypotension refractory to norepinephrine. It is not a first-line agent — norepinephrine must be initiated and proven insufficient before adding vasopressin.
  • Fixed-rate infusion: Per LFN 12.83, the dose is 0.03 units/min — a single fixed rate. Unlike norepinephrine (which is titrated q 3–5 min), there is no titration guidance for vasopressin in the LFN formulary.
  • Recommended mixture: 20 units in 100 mL. At 0.03 units/min, the infusion rate can be calculated from this concentration. Ensure accurate infusion pump programming.
  • Non-catecholamine mechanism: Vasopressin acts on V1 receptors — a different receptor pathway than catecholamines (norepinephrine, epinephrine). This makes it effective as an adjunct when adrenergic receptor responsiveness is diminished in refractory shock.
  • Drug interactions: Alcohol, demeclocycline, epinephrine, heparin, lithium, and phenytoin may decrease the antidiuretic effects of vasopressin.
  • No LFN pediatric standing order: No pediatric dosing established per LFN protocol.

💡 Clinical Context — Why Add Vasopressin?

In prolonged or severe shock, adrenergic receptor downregulation can reduce the effectiveness of catecholamine vasopressors like norepinephrine. Vasopressin acts through a completely different receptor pathway (V1 receptors on vascular smooth muscle), providing vasoconstriction independent of adrenergic signaling. This complementary mechanism makes vasopressin effective when catecholamines alone are insufficient. Per Norepi 12.63 SC #2a: after vasopressin is added, norepinephrine may exceed its listed max rate of 45 mcg/min.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity to vasopressin or any component; chronic nephritis.

⚠️ Caution — High-Risk Populations

Use with caution in patients with: epilepsy; asthma; heart failure; renal disease; elderly patients; children.

⚠️ Adverse Reactions

Angina; hypertension. Vasopressin causes vasoconstriction which can increase myocardial oxygen demand (precipitating angina) and elevate blood pressure. Monitor cardiac rhythm and blood pressure closely during infusion.

⚠️ Drug Interactions

The following agents may decrease the antidiuretic effects of vasopressin: alcohol, demeclocycline, epinephrine, heparin, lithium, and phenytoin.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Vasopressin may produce tonic uterine contractions. Vasopressin clearance may be increased in the second and third trimesters due to pregnancy-induced physiologic changes.

👶 Pediatric

No LFN Standing Order for pediatric vasopressin. Additionally, children are listed under Warnings/Precautions as a population requiring caution.
