🔬 Mechanism of Action

Causes significant increase in myocardial contractility and in ventricular automaticity. Same MOA as calcium chloride — the difference is in delivery: calcium gluconate releases ionized Ca²⁺ more gradually. In hyperkalemia: stabilizes cardiac membrane. In BB/CCB OD: mass action overcomes blockade. In MgSO₄ toxicity: calcium directly antagonizes magnesium's cardiac effects. In MTP: replaces citrate-chelated calcium.

🤰 Pregnancy

Antidotes should be administered to pregnant women if there is a clear indication and should not be withheld because of fears of teratogenicity. Follow current ACLS guidelines. MgSO₄ toxicity antidote role is especially relevant in OB patients receiving magnesium for eclampsia/preeclampsia.

📋 No Numbered Special Considerations in LFN 12.12

Unlike CaCl₂ (12.11) which has 4 numbered SCs, calcium gluconate has no SCs listed. However, the dosing table contains critical clinical context built into each indication. Key application notes:

⚡ HYPERKALEMIA — Requires ECG Changes (Not Just High K⁺)

The LFN indication is Hyperkalemia w/ECG changes specifically — not just elevated K⁺. Must have: peaked T-waves, wide QRS, and/or ventricular ectopy. Dose: 3 grams over 2 minutes (adult). This is a higher dose given faster than some other calcium gluconate indications. Does NOT lower K⁺ — stabilizes the membrane while you implement definitive treatments.

💊 BB/CCB OD — Up to 9 Grams Total (3 × 3 g doses)

3 grams SIVP, repeat q 15 min PRN to a total of 3 doses = up to 9 grams total. This is the highest total calcium gluconate dose in the formulary. BB/CCB OD is the only indication where gluconate is used but CaCl₂ 12.11 does NOT list beta-blocker OD. Know this distinction. Peds: No LFN Standing Order. Full OD treatment ladder: calcium → atropine → glucagon (12.34) → high-dose insulin (12.40) → vasopressors.

💉 MgSO₄ TOXICITY — The Magnesium Antidote

1 gram over 3–5 minutes, may repeat PRN. Calcium directly antagonizes magnesium's effects on the heart and neuromuscular junction. Most common context: OB patients on MgSO₄ for eclampsia/preeclampsia who develop toxicity (loss of DTRs, respiratory depression, bradycardia). Signs to watch: absent patellar reflexes → respiratory depression → cardiac arrest. Peds: 10–30 mg/kg over 2 min.

🩸 MTP — Per PCG 8.02 Blood Product Administration

1–2 grams over 5 minutes. Consider after 1–2 units, include any PTA. PCG 8.02 specifically references calcium gluconate for blood product administration. Citrate anticoagulant chelates ionized calcium → hypocalcemia → ↓ contractility + coagulopathy. Peds: 50 mg/kg over 5 min. Don't wait for symptomatic hypocalcemia — give empirically during MTP.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Ventricular fibrillation — same CI as CaCl₂. Exception: VF caused by hyperK, hypoCa, or CCB/BB toxicity.
2. Hypercalcemia — adding calcium when levels already elevated = cardiac arrest risk.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Patients on digitalis — calcium potentiates digoxin toxicity. Calcium gluconate's slower Ca²⁺ release may be somewhat safer than CaCl₂ in digitalized patients, but still use with caution.
Renal or cardiac insufficiency — impaired calcium clearance.
Immobilized patients — hypercalcemia risk from bone resorption.
High concentrations reaching the heart can cause fatal cardiac arrest — give at specified rates.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (6)

Tingling sensations; hypotension; bradycardia and other dysrhythmias; syncope; cardiac arrest; local reactions. Same adverse profile as CaCl₂. Local reactions are less severe with gluconate than chloride (less caustic to peripheral veins), but extravasation still requires attention.
