⚠️ ORAL MAINTENANCE — 68 Hours per LFN (not "17 doses")

LFN 12.57 specifies oral maintenance as 70 mg/kg q4h for 68 hours. Some references describe this as 17 doses (68 hr ÷ 4 hr = 17), but the LFN protocol is expressed as a duration. Know the LFN wording. IV protocol is 21 hours total; oral is 68 hours + loading dose.

🔬 Correct Mechanism of Action (Antidote Function)

NAC is a glutathione precursor and replenisher. In acetaminophen overdose, the toxic metabolite NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine) depletes hepatic glutathione stores. NAC provides cysteine for glutathione synthesis, restoring the liver's ability to detoxify NAPQI before it causes hepatocellular necrosis.

🤰 Pregnancy

Acetylcysteine crosses the placenta. May be used to treat acetaminophen overdose during pregnancy; IV administration may be preferred by some clinicians. Delaying treatment to pregnant patients with acetaminophen toxicity may increase the risk of adverse maternal and fetal outcomes.

🕐 Timing Is Critical — Most Effective Within 8 Hours

NAC is most protective when started before glutathione is fully depleted. The window of highest efficacy is within 8 hours of ingestion. However, benefit continues beyond 8 hours and even into late presentations with established hepatotoxicity via anti-inflammatory and microcirculatory mechanisms. Do not withhold NAC due to late presentation.

🟦 Oral vs IV Route — Key Differences

Oral (Mucomyst): 140 mg/kg loading, 70 mg/kg q4h × 68 hr. Mix with juice/soft drink. Nausea is common due to sulfur smell. NG/OG tube acceptable.

IV (Parenteral): Three-bag 21-hour protocol (150 → 50 → 100 mg/kg in D5W). Anaphylactoid reactions in ~10–20%; most mild. Most common during loading dose.

⚠️ ANAPHYLACTOID REACTION TO IV NAC — NOT TRUE ALLERGY
  • Anaphylactoid reactions are NOT IgE-mediated — they result from direct histamine release, not true allergy.
  • Most common during the loading dose (Bag 1); symptoms: urticaria, flushing, bronchospasm, angioedema.
  • Management: Stop infusion → treat symptoms → restart at slower rate. Do NOT permanently discontinue NAC.
  • Risk factors: Lower APAP levels (less NAPQI to consume NAC), asthma history, rapid infusion rate.
  • Per LFN warnings: use caution in patients with asthma or bronchospasm — increased risk of anaphylactoid reaction severity.
🚫 CONTRAINDICATION (1)

Hypersensitivity to acetylcysteine or any component of the formulation.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (4)

1. Asthma — IV NAC may provoke bronchospasm; have albuterol available; consider slower infusion rate.

2. Bronchospasm — same concern as asthma; anaphylactoid risk during IV loading is higher in patients with reactive airway disease.

3. Upper GI bleeding risk (oral route) — oral NAC can irritate GI mucosa; use IV route when GI bleeding is a concern.

4. Sodium restriction — the IV formulation contains sodium as the preservative sodium edetate; relevant in patients on strict sodium restriction.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (LFN 12.57 — 9 Listed)

Nausea/vomiting; urticarial; tachycardia; rash; pruritus; flushing; URI symptoms; stomatitis; fever; drowsiness.

• Nausea/vomiting — most common with oral route (sulfur odor); antiemetic pretreatment recommended
• Urticaria, rash, pruritus, flushing — anaphylactoid reaction during IV infusion
• Tachycardia — component of anaphylactoid reaction
• URI symptoms — mucolytic effect may loosen secretions
• Stomatitis — oral route GI side effect
• Fever, drowsiness — systemic effects
