🔵 Adult vs Peds Dosing Structure — Key Differences

Adult has two distinct scenarios in the formulary table: (1) initiating propofol — bolus 1 mg/kg (max 25 mg) then 25 mcg/kg/min, and (2) already receiving propofol with inadequate sedation — bolus 10–20 mg (flat, not weight-based) then increase rate by 10 mcg/kg/min.

Peds has one entry covering both situations: "To initiate infusion or if patient is inadequately sedated" — bolus 0.5 mg/kg (max 20 mg) then 25 mcg/kg/min. The adult rescue bolus is a flat 10–20 mg; the peds bolus is always weight-based (0.5 mg/kg).

The max infusion rate (75 mcg/kg/min) is identical for adult and peds.

🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Propofol causes global CNS depression, presumably as an agonist of GABAA receptors and perhaps reduced glutamatergic activity through NMDA receptor blockade."

📝 MOA in Clinical Context

LFN 12.72 describes a two-mechanism MOA: (1) GABAA receptor agonism — propofol enhances inhibitory GABA signaling, producing CNS depression; (2) reduced glutamatergic activity via NMDA receptor blockade — the excitatory glutamate system is dampened. Together these produce "global CNS depression." The word "presumably" and "perhaps" are in the LFN text — acknowledging mechanistic uncertainty in the literature.

The category is "General anesthetic, sedative" — both terms appear, reflecting its dual role: induction agent in anesthesia and sedation infusion in the ICU/transport setting. LFN 12.72 uses it only for post-intubation sedation.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

"Propofol crosses the placenta and may be associated with neonatal CNS and respiratory depression. However, when indicated, its use should not be avoided."

The LFN pregnancy framework: acknowledge risk (neonatal CNS and respiratory depression from placental transfer) then override with indication-driven use. The phrase "should not be avoided" is an active endorsement when clinically indicated. This is similar to the antidote pregnancy framework but applied to an anesthetic agent.

📋 Special Considerations (4)
1
IV boluses MUST be given via pump, not IV push. The word "MUST" is capitalized in the LFN 12.72 text. This is the strongest directive language in the SCs — an absolute requirement, not a preference. Applies to every propofol bolus regardless of clinical urgency.
2
Propofol lacks analgesic properties, thus analgesia administration is usually required and will generally allow for a lower infusion dose. Two distinct points: (a) no analgesia — separate pain management required; (b) adequate analgesia reduces the propofol dose needed. PCG 2.08 echoes: "ensure pain management is provided for patient comfort."
3
Effective in-hospital dosages may provide insufficient sedation during transport. A transport-specific consideration. The stimulation of transport (vibration, noise, movement, patient anxiety) may require higher doses than what was adequate in the ICU. Flight crew should anticipate sedation needs proactively at handoff.
4
Fluids and/or vasopressors may be required during propofol administration to prevent hypotension. Hypotension is both a warning and an adverse reaction — SC #4 specifies the proactive management strategy: fluids AND/OR vasopressors. The conjunction "and/or" means either or both may be required depending on the clinical picture.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATION — Hypersensitivity (3 components)

Hypersensitivity to the drug, eggs, or soy products.

The CI is a single entry but covers three hypersensitivity sources: the drug itself, egg products, and soy products. This reflects propofol's formulation: it is an oil-in-water emulsion containing soybean oil and egg lecithin as excipients. Allergy to any of the three components is a contraindication.

This is one of only a few LFN formulary drugs where the CI extends to food allergens. The egg and soy components are part of the drug vehicle, not the active molecule itself — but LFN 12.72 treats all three as CI-level hypersensitivity.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (1 — with 3 specific risks named)

"Administer carefully, have resuscitation equipment at the bedside. This drug can cause apnea, hypoventilation, and hypotension."

The warning is one statement with two directives and three named risks:
• Administer carefully — general caution
• Have resuscitation equipment at the bedside — preparation requirement
• Apnea — respiratory arrest possible
• Hypoventilation — partial respiratory depression
• Hypotension — cardiovascular depression

All three risks (apnea, hypoventilation, hypotension) also appear in the adverse reactions section, creating overlap — but they are specifically enumerated in the warning to justify the "resuscitation equipment at the bedside" directive.
