⚖️ Adult vs Peds DKA — Key Difference

Adults have three tiers (>5.4 / 4.0–5.4 / <4.0). Peds have only two tiers (>5.4 / ≤5.4). The peds ≤5.4 threshold gets 40 mEq/L regardless of the exact level — no 20 mEq/L mid-tier for peds. Peds also has an explicit max infusion rate: 0.5 mEq/kg/hr. No separate infusion rate cap is listed for adults in the DKA column.

🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Potassium is the chief cation of body cells. A deficiency of either potassium or chloride will lead to a deficit of the other."

📝 MOA in Clinical Context

LFN 12.69 states potassium is the chief cation of body cells — the primary intracellular positive ion. The second half of the MOA ("a deficiency of either potassium or chloride will lead to a deficit of the other") explains the electrolyte coupling: treating hypokalemia alone with KCl also addresses concurrent chloride depletion, and vice versa. This bidirectional dependency is the basis for using potassium chloride (not just potassium) as the replacement form. The MOA does not mention cardiac conduction, membrane potential, or enzyme systems — the LFN entry is deliberately simple.

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

"Potassium requirements are the same in pregnant and nonpregnant women. Potassium supplementation (that does not cause maternal hyperkalemia) would not be expected to cause adverse fetal events."

Key points: (1) No altered dosing needed in pregnancy — requirements are the same. (2) The safety qualifier is important: supplementation is safe as long as it does not cause maternal hyperkalemia. The risk to the fetus is from over-replacement (maternal hyperkalemia) rather than from KCl itself.

⚖️ DKA Tier Decision Logic — Adult

The three-tier adult DKA system reflects the physiology of DKA: insulin shifts potassium into cells, dropping serum K+. The tier system preemptively supplements based on where K+ will go as DKA is treated, not just where it is now. K+ >5.4 = no KCl yet (safe room to fall as insulin works). K+ 4.0–5.4 = low-dose supplement to keep pace with cellular uptake. K+ <4.0 = aggressive supplement because the patient is already in the danger zone before insulin drives it lower.

For peds, LFN simplifies to two tiers — once K+ is at or below 5.4, use 40 mEq/L. The additional precision of the adult 20 mEq/L mid-tier is absent.

💊 Hypokalemia IV vs Oral Threshold — Exact LFN Values

Adult oral: K+ 2.5–3.5 → 40 mEq PO, repeat ×1 at 30–60 min. Adult IV: K+ <2.5 asymptomatic OR <3 symptomatic → 40 mEq IV, max 10 mEq/hr.

The adult oral ceiling (3.5) is broader than the peds oral range (2.5–3.0). For peds, the oral range tops out at 3.0. The IV threshold is identical for adults and peds: <2.5 asymptomatic or <3 symptomatic. Only the dose differs: adult = 40 mEq flat; peds = 0.5 mEq/kg over 1–2 hours.

This cross-protocol relationship is central to DKA management. LFN 12.40 (Insulin) explicitly states: "Potassium must be ≥ 3.3 mEq/L before administration." KCl 12.69 provides the correction protocol.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

"Diseases where high potassium levels may be encountered, in patients with hyperkalemia, renal failure and in conditions in which potassium retention is present, or where additives of potassium and chloride could be clinically detrimental."

And: "Never give potassium IV push."

The IV push prohibition is embedded in the CI section — not just a warning — making it a hard stop. Hyperkalemia and renal failure are the clinical contexts where additional potassium is contraindicated.

⚠️ Warning / Precaution

"Never administer more than 20 mEq/hr through a central line or more than 10 mEq/hr through a peripheral line."

LFN 12.69 has one warning — the rate caps by access type. Central = max 20 mEq/hr. Peripheral = max 10 mEq/hr. No additional language about continuous ECG monitoring or cardiac monitoring requirements is stated in LFN 12.69 (though clinically appropriate).

⚠️ Adverse Reactions

Monitor IV site for pain.

LFN 12.69 lists only one adverse reaction: monitor IV site for pain. This reflects the tissue irritability of concentrated potassium solutions. Phlebitis, burning, and infiltration are the local concerns. No systemic adverse reactions are listed beyond the CI/warning framework (hyperkalemia, cardiac effects from IV push are covered in the CI section).
