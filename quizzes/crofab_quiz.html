🔬 Mechanism of Action

Binds to crotalid venom toxins. Contains venom-specific F(ab’)2 fragments of immunoglobulin G (IgG) that bind and neutralize venom toxins of North American pit vipers (rattlesnakes), facilitating redistribution away from target tissues and elimination from the body. Derived from sheep immunized with venom from four pit viper species: Western Diamondback, Eastern Diamondback, Mojave rattlesnake, and Cottonmouth.

🤰 Pregnancy

Information related to use in pregnancy is limited. Antidotes should be administered to pregnant patients if there is a clear indication and should not be withheld because of fears of teratogenicity. A symptomatic pit viper envenomation in a pregnant patient is a clear indication for CroFab.

  • Initial dosing: monitor for up to 1 hour following the infusion to determine if initial control has been achieved as evidenced by the arrest of local signs of envenomation (e.g., leading edge of local injury is not progressing). Some clinicians recommend 8–12 vials for patients with immediately life-threatening effects (shock, serious active bleeding). Repeat with additional 4–6 vials if control not achieved. Continue until local manifestations, coagulation tests, and systemic signs are normal. Max initial dose: 12 vials.
  • Maintenance dosing: once initial control is achieved, administer 2 vials every 6 hours for up to 18 hours. Optimal dosing beyond 18 hours has not been established; however, treatment may be continued if deemed necessary based on patient condition.
  • Antivenin dose reflects venom size rather than patient size; therefore, the FDA recommends the same initial and subsequent doses for adult and pediatric patients regardless of age or weight.
  • Initiate therapy as soon as possible and preferably within 6 hours after a North American pit viper bite in patients exhibiting any signs of envenomation. Contact local poison control center or clinical toxicologist for consultation.
  • Although tourniquets are not recommended, antivenin therapy should be initiated prior to tourniquet removal in patients who present with one in place. Tourniquet removal should only occur after the patient is tolerating the full infusion rate without signs of anaphylaxis.
📋 INITIAL CONTROL — What It Looks Like

"Initial control" = arrest of local signs of envenomation. The leading edge of swelling should stop advancing. Mark the leading edge with a pen and timestamp. If it hasn’t progressed in 1 hour after infusion completes, initial control is achieved. Also requires: improving coagulation parameters and resolving systemic signs (hypotension, coagulopathy, neurologic effects).

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS

Hypersensitivity to drug/class/compound.
Hypersensitivity to papain and/or papaya — UNLESS benefit outweighs risks AND appropriate management for anaphylaxis is readily available. (Papain is used in the manufacturing process to cleave antibodies into Fab fragments.)

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Caution if hypersensitive to latex/dust mites, or previous CroFab use. Repeat exposure increases sensitization risk. Latex cross-reactivity relates to shared plant protein epitopes. Have epinephrine, airway equipment, and IV fluids immediately available during infusion.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (13+)

Anaphylactoid reaction; anaphylaxis; serum sickness; hypersensitivity reaction (delayed); urticaria; rash; pruritus; nausea; back pain; subcutaneous nodule; cough; anorexia; myalgia; rigors; chest pain; nervousness. Serum sickness is a delayed reaction (7–14 days post-treatment) causing fever, joint pain, rash, and lymphadenopathy — patients should be warned to seek care if these symptoms develop.
