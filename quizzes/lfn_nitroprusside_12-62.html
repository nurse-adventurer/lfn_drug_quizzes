🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Animal studies have shown that nitroprusside may cross the placental barrier and result in fetal cyanide levels that are dose-related to maternal nitroprusside levels. However, information related to use in pregnancy is limited.

Pregnancy/lactation is listed as a Warning/Precaution in LFN 12.62 — the fetal cyanide risk from SC #3 extends to the fetus, compounding the pregnancy concern.

⚗️ SC #1 — D5W Only: Why?

Nitroprusside degrades in chloride-containing solutions such as NS. It must be mixed exclusively in D5W. Using NS or other saline-based diluents can cause degradation of the nitroprusside molecule, reducing potency and potentially altering the drug's chemistry. Per LFN 12.62 SC #1: "Mix with D5W only."

🫙 SC #2 — Light Protection: Why?

Nitroprusside is highly photosensitive. Exposure to light degrades the drug rapidly through photolysis, releasing free cyanide. Wrap the bag and tubing in foil or other opaque material immediately upon preparation. Even brief light exposure can degrade the solution — the characteristic color change (from light orange/red to dark brown or blue) indicates degradation; discard if color changes occur.

☠️ SC #3 — Cyanide Toxicity: The Critical Concern

Each nitroprusside molecule releases five cyanide ions upon metabolic degradation. At therapeutic doses and short durations, the liver converts cyanide to thiocyanate (renally excreted) fast enough to prevent accumulation. Prolonged use or high doses overwhelm this conversion capacity.

  • Risk factors: High doses (approaching 10 mcg/kg/min), prolonged infusion duration, hepatic impairment, renal insufficiency (thiocyanate accumulates)
  • Signs of cyanide toxicity: Lactic acidosis (cells cannot use oxygen), altered mental status, tachycardia, bright red venous blood (oxyhemoglobin — cyanide blocks cytochrome oxidase)
  • Thiocyanate toxicity (slower): Confusion, psychosis, tinnitus, seizures — especially with renal failure
  • Mitigation: Use lowest effective dose; monitor; avoid prolonged high-rate infusions
  • Weight-based dosing: Nitroprusside is dosed in mcg/kg/min — unlike nicardipine (mg/hr) or nitroglycerin (mcg/min). Requires patient weight for accurate dosing and rate calculation.
  • 1–10 min duration: The ultra-short offset is what makes nitroprusside useful for precise titration — but also dangerous if over-titrated. BP returns to baseline within minutes of stopping, allowing rapid correction of overshoot hypotension.
  • Metoprolol interaction (LFN 12.53 SC #4): "May enhance hypotensive effects of loop diuretics, nifedipine, and nitroprusside." A patient on metoprolol receiving nitroprusside requires careful monitoring — blunted sympathetic compensation amplifies hypotension risk.
  • Renal impairment — dual concern: (1) Warnings include renal insufficiency directly. (2) Thiocyanate (cyanide metabolite) is renally excreted — impaired kidneys allow thiocyanate accumulation, increasing toxicity risk even at doses that would be safe in normal renal function.
🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2 — both specific and non-obvious)

1. Compensatory hypertension — as in AV shunt or coarctation of the aorta:
In compensatory hypertension, elevated BP is the physiologic mechanism maintaining perfusion despite an underlying obstruction or shunt. Forcing BP down with nitroprusside removes this compensatory response → precipitous drop in perfusion to vital organs. LFN 12.62 explicitly names AV shunt and coarctation of the aorta as examples.

2. Control of hypertension in patients with inadequate cerebral circulation:
Patients with inadequate cerebral circulation (severe cerebrovascular disease, cerebral vasospasm) rely on systemic pressure to maintain CPP. Vasodilation → ↓ MAP → ↓ CPP → cerebral ischemia. This is a distinct concern from general hypertensive patients where BP reduction is the goal.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (2)

1. Hepatic insufficiency: The liver is responsible for converting cyanide to thiocyanate. Impaired hepatic function reduces this conversion → cyanide accumulates faster at any given dose. Cyanide toxicity risk is substantially elevated in liver disease.

2. Renal insufficiency / pregnancy / lactation: Renal insufficiency impairs thiocyanate clearance (accumulation risk). Pregnancy/lactation: fetal cyanide levels are dose-related to maternal levels (per pregnancy note); use is a significant concern.

Both bradycardia and tachycardia are listed as a single adverse reaction entry. Tachycardia is the reflex response to arterial vasodilation and ↓ BP (baroreceptor activation). Bradycardia can result from overshoot hypotension or a vasovagal response. The dual listing reflects that either direction of rate change is possible.
