🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Direct-acting sympathomimetic amine identical to body catecholamine norepinephrine. It acts directly and predominantly on alpha-adrenergic receptors; little action on beta receptors except in heart (beta1 receptors). Its main therapeutic effects are vasoconstriction and very slight cardiac stimulation."

Alpha Receptors — PREDOMINANT
🎯 Primarily alpha-adrenergic
→ Vasoconstriction (main effect)
→ ↑ SVR → ↑ MAP
→ Arterial and venous constriction

This is the primary mechanism behind its use in distributive shock — restores vascular tone lost from pathologic vasodilation
Beta Receptors — LIMITED
🎯 Little beta activity
→ Beta1 in heart only
→ Very slight cardiac stimulation
→ Mild ↑ contractility
→ No significant beta2 effect

The contrast with epinephrine (strong alpha + strong beta1 + beta2) is a key pharmacologic distinction

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

Norepinephrine crosses the placenta. Norepinephrine may be considered; however, the effects of vasoactive medications on the fetus should also be considered.

The pregnancy note is notably cautious — it allows for use but emphasizes fetal hemodynamic impact. Uterine vasculature constriction from alpha stimulation can reduce uteroplacental blood flow. This is relevant when treating maternal shock.

🩸 SC #1 — Large, Stable Vein Requirement

Per LFN 12.63 SC #1: "Must be infused through a large, stable vein to avoid necrosis."

Norepinephrine is a potent vasoconstrictor. Extravasation into surrounding tissue causes intense local vasoconstriction → ischemia → tissue necrosis. This is not a theoretical concern — it is a well-documented complication of peripheral norepinephrine administration and the reason central venous access is preferred in hospital settings.

In transport, where central access may not be available, use the largest caliber, most stable peripheral IV site available. Monitor the site continuously for signs of infiltration: blanching, pallor, pain, swelling. If extravasation is suspected, stop the infusion immediately.

📈 SC #2 — Escalation Rules: The 30 mcg/min Threshold and Max Exceedance

LFN 12.63 SC #2 establishes a two-part escalation framework:

Part A: "The dose range listed for infusions is your starting place, titrate as needed to maintain the patient's blood pressure. Consider adding additional agents when approaching the listed max dose."

→ This means at >30 mcg/min, begin considering a second vasopressor rather than pushing to the 45 mcg/min max alone.

Part B: "After additional vasopressors (epinephrine, vasopressin, amiodarone) are administered, you may exceed the listed max rate of 45 mcg/min."

→ The 45 mcg/min max is not an absolute ceiling — it is a threshold for escalation to combination therapy. Once additional vasopressors are on board, the norepinephrine rate can be increased further.

  • Vasopressin (12.83) is the named second-line agent for norepinephrine-refractory shock: LFN 12.83 indication is explicitly "Shock/hypotension refractory to norepinephrine." This is the primary named escalation when norepinephrine is not achieving BP goals.
  • Dual adult dosing options: 2–10 mcg/min (flat, non-weight-based) OR 0.05 mcg/kg/min (weight-based). The flat mcg/min option allows rapid clinical use without weight calculation. The weight-based option allows consistent dosing proportional to patient size. Both are valid starting approaches per LFN 12.63.
  • Peds max differs from adult max structure: Adult max = 45 mcg/min OR 2 mcg/kg/min. Peds max = 45 mcg/min only — no mcg/kg/min maximum listed in the peds column. Peds also has no "consider second agent at 30 mcg/min" notation in the peds column (though SC #2 guidance applies conceptually).
  • Reflex bradycardia as adverse reaction: Alpha-mediated vasoconstriction → ↑ MAP → baroreceptors detect elevated BP → vagal response → ↓ HR. This is mechanistically the opposite of what you might expect from a vasopressor. If HR drops precipitously during norepinephrine infusion, reflex bradycardia from effective BP restoration is the likely cause.
🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (1)

Hypersensitivity to norepinephrine or any component. This is the only listed CI — notably, there are no hemodynamic contraindications (no equivalent of nitroprusside's "compensatory hypertension" CI). In true shock, the risk of withholding the drug far outweighs hypersensitivity concerns unless a prior reaction is documented.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions (6)
Hypertension
Potent pressor — may overshoot in patients with existing HTN
Hyperthyroidism
Thyroid hormone potentiates catecholamine effects → amplified response
Severe Heart Disease
↑ afterload may worsen LV function; use with caution in LV failure
Elderly Patients
Increased sensitivity; higher risk of end-organ ischemia from vasoconstriction
MAO Inhibitors (within 14 days)
MAOIs block NE breakdown → markedly prolonged, enhanced pressor response
Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)
Block NE reuptake → enhanced pressor effect; risk of severe hypertension

MAO inhibitor and TCA warnings are pharmacokinetic drug interactions, not just clinical caution flags. Both mechanisms increase norepinephrine's effect at the synapse — MAOIs by preventing breakdown, TCAs by blocking reuptake. The "within 14 days" time frame for MAOIs reflects the long duration of irreversible MAO inhibition even after the drug is discontinued.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (3)
Hypertension
From excessive vasoconstriction; monitor BP continuously; titrate down if overshoot
Reflex Bradycardia
Alpha-mediated ↑ MAP → baroreceptor-mediated vagal slowing; paradoxical HR ↓ as BP normalizes
Tissue Necrosis with Extravasation
Local vasoconstriction → ischemia → necrosis; requires large, stable vein (SC #1)

⚠️ Key Points

  • CI (1): Hypersensitivity only
  • Warnings (6): Hypertension; hyperthyroidism; severe heart disease; elderly; within 14 days of MAOI therapy; TCAs
  • Adverse (3): Hypertension; reflex bradycardia; tissue necrosis with extravasation
  • MOA: Predominantly alpha → vasoconstriction; little beta except beta1 in heart (very slight cardiac stimulation)
  • SC #1: Large, stable vein required — extravasation = necrosis
  • SC #2: Consider 2nd agent at >30 mcg/min; after epi/vasopressin/amiodarone, may exceed 45 mcg/min
  • Named escalation drug: Vasopressin 12.83 — indication is "shock refractory to norepinephrine"
  • MAOI interaction: 14-day wash-out period — MAOIs block NE breakdown → amplified pressor effect
  • TCA interaction: Block NE reuptake → enhanced pressor effect
  • Reflex bradycardia: Alpha → ↑ MAP → baroreceptor → vagal ↓ HR — paradoxical but expected
