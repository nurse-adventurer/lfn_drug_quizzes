🔬 Mechanism of Action

Inhibits calcium ion influx through slow channels into cell of myocardial and arterial smooth muscle. Slows SA and AV node conduction without affecting normal arterial action potential or intravascular conduction. As a non-dihydropyridine CCB, diltiazem has BOTH cardiac effects (SA/AV node slowing, negative inotropy) AND vascular effects (vasodilation). This distinguishes it from dihydropyridine CCBs (clevidipine, nicardipine, nifedipine) which are primarily vascular-selective.

🤰 Pregnancy

When treatment of chronic hypertension during pregnancy is indicated, agents other than diltiazem may be preferred. Chronic maternal hypertension may increase risk of birth defects. Untreated chronic hypertension also risks adverse maternal outcomes.

  • Consider physiologic reasons for the patient's tachycardia before administering, such as heart failure, hypovolemia, or pain. Compensatory sinus tachycardia should NOT be treated with rate-slowing agents — treating the cause (fluids for hypovolemia, pain management, antibiotics for sepsis) is appropriate. Slowing a compensatory tachycardia can cause hemodynamic collapse.
🚨 SC #1 — THE MOST IMPORTANT CONSIDERATION

Before every diltiazem dose, ask: "Is this tachycardia the problem, or is it compensating for a bigger problem?" A patient with fever 39.2°C, HR 140, BP 88/50 doesn't need rate control — they need fluid resuscitation and infection treatment. The tachycardia is HELPING maintain cardiac output. Diltiazem in this scenario can be lethal.

📋 DOSE CALCULATION EXAMPLES

80 kg patient, 1st bolus: 80 × 0.25 = 20 mg (exactly at the max)
80 kg patient, 2nd bolus: 80 × 0.35 = 28 mg → give 20 mg (capped at max)
60 kg patient, 1st bolus: 60 × 0.25 = 15 mg (below max)
60 kg patient, 2nd bolus: 60 × 0.35 = 21 mg → give 20 mg (capped)

At mixture 100 mg/100 mL (1 mg/mL): infusion at 10 mg/hr = 10 mL/hr. Simple.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (8) — Extensive List

1. Hypersensitivity
2. Sick sinus syndrome
3. 2nd or 3rd degree AV blocks
4. Acute MI or pulmonary congestion
5. Severe hypotension
6. Cardiogenic shock
7. Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome (AV nodal blockade → accessory pathway acceleration → VF)
8. Ventricular tachycardia (QRS >0.12 seconds) (VT requires amiodarone/cardioversion, not AV nodal blockade)

🚨 CRITICAL — Do NOT Use with IV Beta-Blockers

Should not be used with IV beta blockers due to increased risk of CHF. Combined AV nodal blockade + negative inotropy from BOTH drug classes → severe bradycardia, heart block, hypotension, acute heart failure. Exception: aortic emergencies where rate control is critical despite beta-blocker therapy (per LFN aortic dosing).

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Caution in conduction abnormalities; renal/hepatic impairment; elderly; nursing mothers. May increase digoxin and/or quinidine levels (drug interaction — can cause digoxin toxicity).

🚫 Adverse Reactions (6)

Headache; fatigue; bradycardia; hypotension; syncope; palpitations; nausea. Bradycardia and hypotension are the most clinically significant. Manage hypotension with fluids and consider IV calcium (gluconate or chloride) for significant CCB-induced hemodynamic effects.
