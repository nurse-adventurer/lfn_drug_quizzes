🔬 Mechanism of Action (LFN verbatim)

"Reduces the activity of the vagus nerve. This activates the vomiting center in the medulla oblongata and blocks serotonin receptors in the chemoreceptor trigger zone."

CTZ Site
5-HT3 receptors in chemoreceptor trigger zone detect emetogenic signals (drugs, toxins, metabolic)
Vagal Site
5-HT3 receptors on vagal afferents in GI tract detect local stimuli (gut distension, injury)
Ondansetron
Selectively blocks 5-HT3 receptors at both sites → reduces serotonin-mediated nausea signaling
Vomiting Center
Receives fewer emetic signals → ↓ vomiting reflex → antiemetic effect

🔵 5-HT3 Antagonist — Why "Selective"?

Ondansetron selectively blocks 5-HT3 (serotonin type 3) receptors — the subtype specifically located in the CTZ and vagal afferents. It does NOT block dopamine receptors (like droperidol), histamine receptors (like diphenhydramine/promethazine), or muscarinic receptors. This selectivity explains why ondansetron has no extrapyramidal effects at therapeutic doses compared to dopamine antagonists — and why it's listed as the drug of choice for dystonic reactions caused by promethazine (PCG 2.05 Pearl #4).

🤰 Pregnancy Considerations

🚫 Avoid — <10 Weeks Gestation
Listed as a contraindication in LFN 12.65. First trimester risk is under study — congenital malformation risk has not been definitively established. The "under study" language indicates ongoing research, not clearance.
✅ May Consider — Severe/Refractory NVP
"Ondansetron may be considered for the treatment of severe or refractory nausea and vomiting of pregnancy (NVP) when preferred agents have failed." After first trimester, risk-benefit may favor use if other antiemetics have been ineffective.
💡 SC #1 — Nausea vs Active Vomiting

Per LFN 12.65 SC #1: "Works best to prevent or treat current nausea but is not as effective for active vomiting."

Mechanistic explanation: Ondansetron blocks the serotonin signal that triggers the vomiting reflex. Once the vomiting center has been activated and the act of vomiting is already in progress, the 5-HT3 receptor blockade cannot suppress the reflex in real time. The drug is most effective when given early — before the vomiting cascade is fully activated — or prophylactically to prevent nausea from escalating to vomiting.

Clinical implication: If the patient is actively retching, ondansetron may still be given (it will help as the current episode resolves and prevent recurrence), but the crew should not expect it to immediately stop active vomiting.

⚡ PCG 2.05 — "Slow IVP Over 2 Minutes"

PCG 2.05 specifies that ondansetron should be given as a slow IVP over 2 minutes for both adult and pediatric patients when given IV. This is consistent with the adverse reaction profile — rapid IV bolus of ondansetron is associated with increased risk of QT prolongation and cardiac effects. The slow push reduces the peak concentration spike and the associated cardiac conduction risk.

IM route bypasses this concern — IM administration produces a slower rise in plasma concentration and is appropriate when IV access is not established or when the slow push is impractical.

⚕️ PCG 2.05 Pearl — Ondansetron as Treatment for Promethazine-Induced Dystonia

PCG 2.05 Pearl #4 explicitly states: "Drug of choice for dystonic reactions due to promethazine."

Mechanism: Promethazine is a dopamine antagonist. Dopamine antagonism in the nigrostriatal pathway → extrapyramidal effects → acute dystonia (muscle rigidity, torticollis, oculogyric crisis). Diphenhydramine (anticholinergic) is the most commonly cited treatment, but per LFN PCG 2.05, ondansetron is specified as the drug of choice for promethazine-induced dystonic reactions.

This is a cross-drug pearl — ondansetron is used to manage a side effect caused by one of the drugs below it in the same antiemetic treatment ladder.

👴 PCG 2.05 — Promethazine Combination Caution in Elderly

PCG 2.05 Pearl #3: "Use caution in combination with promethazine in the elderly due to the combined anticholinergic properties of both medications." Listed effects: blurred vision, flushing, urinary retention, constipation, irregular heartbeat, confusion, and memory problems.

Note: While ondansetron is not primarily anticholinergic, it has minor muscarinic activity, and the combination with promethazine (strongly anticholinergic) creates a clinically relevant additive burden in elderly patients who already have decreased anticholinergic tolerance.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (3)

1. Hypersensitivity to ondansetron or other serotonin blockers (class-wide hypersensitivity possible)

2. QT prolongation >500 milliseconds — specific threshold. Ondansetron itself prolongs the QT interval (listed as an adverse reaction). Administering it when the QT is already critically prolonged increases risk of Torsades de Pointes → ventricular fibrillation.

3. Avoid in pregnancy less than 10 weeks gestation — first trimester CI due to potential teratogenicity under study.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

Hepatic disease — reduced ondansetron clearance → drug accumulates → prolonged/enhanced effect and adverse reactions. Relevant given the drug is commonly used in cirrhotic patients (post-variceal bleed nausea, for example).

PCG 2.05 adds expanded warnings: Elderly; asthmatics; impaired hepatic function; COPD; seizure disorders; CNS depression from any chemical depressant. Cardiac monitor must be applied if ondansetron is administered.
