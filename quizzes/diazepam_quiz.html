🔬 Mechanism of Action

Long-acting benzodiazepine. Binds to stereospecific benzodiazepine receptors on the postsynaptic GABA neuron at several sites within the central nervous system, including the limbic system, reticular formation. Enhances GABA's inhibitory effect by increasing chloride channel opening frequency, causing membrane hyperpolarization and reduced neuronal excitability. The "long-acting" distinction matters: duration >12 hours with active metabolites (desmethyldiazepam, half-life 30-200 hr).

🤰 Pregnancy

Diazepam and its metabolites cross the placenta. In-utero exposure has potential to cause harm. Teratogenic effects observed in some studies; however, a clear association not established. Use with caution — for seizure emergencies in pregnancy, the benefit of seizure termination outweighs risk.

  • Diazepam should NEVER be diluted prior to administration due to risk of precipitation. The injectable form is dissolved in propylene glycol/ethanol solvents. Adding aqueous fluids (NS, D5W) disrupts the solvent balance, causing the drug to precipitate as particles. Administer undiluted, directly into the IV port closest to the insertion site.
  • Rapid administration may cause respiratory depression and/or hypotension.
    a. Adults: max rate of 5 mg/min
    b. Pedi: max rate of 2 mg/min
    The propylene glycol vehicle can cause hypotension, bradycardia, and arrhythmias with rapid injection. For a 10 mg dose, allow at least 2 minutes.
  • If extravasation occurs:
    a. Stop IV immediately and disconnect (leave cannula/needle in place)
    b. Gently aspirate extravasated solution (do NOT flush the line)
    c. Remove needle/cannula
    d. Elevate extremity
    e. Apply dry cold or warm compresses
    The propylene glycol vehicle is highly caustic to subcutaneous tissue.
🚨 SC #1 — NEVER DILUTE — Unique to Diazepam

This is specific to diazepam's lipid-soluble formulation in propylene glycol. Midazolam is water-soluble and CAN be diluted. If you see cloudiness or particles in diazepam solution, do NOT administer. This "never dilute" rule is one of the most testable points in the diazepam formulary.

📋 ADMINISTRATION RATE COMPARISON — Adult vs Peds

Adult: max 5 mg/min. For 10 mg = at least 2 minutes.
Peds: max 2 mg/min. For 3.3 mg (0.15 mg/kg × 22 kg) = at least 1.5 minutes.
Slow push reduces respiratory depression, hypotension, and propylene glycol toxicity. Have BVM and flumazenil (12.31) available.

🚫 CONTRAINDICATIONS (2)

1. Hypersensitivity to diazepam or any component of the formulation.
2. Acute narrow-angle glaucoma. Diazepam can cause mydriasis through central mechanisms, which in narrow-angle anatomy pushes the iris against the trabecular meshwork, blocking aqueous humor outflow and precipitating an acute glaucoma attack with severe IOP elevation and potential permanent vision loss. Open-angle glaucoma is NOT a contraindication.

⚠️ Warnings / Precautions

May cause CNS depression and anterograde amnesia. Paradoxical reactions, including hyperactive or aggressive behavior — more common in elderly and pediatric patients. If paradoxical reaction occurs, do NOT give more diazepam; consider alternative agents (droperidol, haloperidol). Use caution with concurrent CNS depressants (opioids, alcohol), hepatic impairment (prolonged metabolism), and respiratory compromise.

🚫 Adverse Reactions (7)

Drowsiness; hypotension; nausea/vomiting; ataxia; nasal discomfort; epistaxis; bradycardia. The nasal discomfort and epistaxis relate to the Valtoco (nasal spray) formulation. Hypotension and bradycardia relate to the propylene glycol vehicle (especially with rapid IV push). Reversal agent: flumazenil (12.31) — 0.2 mg IV, max 1 mg total.
